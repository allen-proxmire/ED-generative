# Remember, or Forget
### How the Arrow of Time Sorts Every Continuum Law, and Draws Its Own Coastline

*Allen Proxmire*

*Series: Event Density, Part 10. Companion paper: `Paper_TheArrowSortsTheContinuum`.*

---

Nine parts of this series have each done the same small thing in a different corner: take a fact physics leaves bare, and hand back a reason. The reason has almost always been the same word, said in a different accent. The arrow. Commitment, irreversible, built into the substrate at the bottom instead of emerging on top.

This part is the one where all of that becomes a single picture. Because the arrow does not just explain spin, or the Born rule, or a black hole's temperature, one at a time. It sorts the *entire* zoo of continuum laws, heat and fluids and electromagnetism and gravity and the rest, into a small number of bins, by one question asked of each. And the honest beauty of it is that the same principle that says which laws Event Density builds also says, sharply and in advance, which laws it *cannot*. A theory that can only say yes is not telling you anything. This one draws its own coastline.

## The ladder nobody mentions

Start with a thing that sounds like bookkeeping and turns out to be the whole trick. When people say a fine-grained world "becomes" a smooth continuum law, they talk as if it is one step. It is not. Nobody goes from atoms straight to the equation for how heat spreads. The real route has a rung in the middle: atoms, then a *kinetic* picture of particles streaming and colliding, then the smooth fluid equations. Two coarse-grainings, not one. This is ordinary kinetic theory, in every textbook.

Event Density's direct coarse-graining lands on the *first* rung. Squint at the substrate once, and what you get is **transport**: things streaming, concentrating, structure being built. You do not get the smooth, spread-out, memoryless laws directly. Those live one rung higher, and getting to them takes a second squint. The entire content of this part is what that second squint adds, why it adds the same thing every single time, and why one primitive decides the whole map.

## The one question: does the law remember?

Here is what the arrow actually writes into the world. Not motion, exactly, since every step a worldline takes is already a commitment. What it writes is **memory**: the correlations between commitments, the direction they share, the phase they carry, the record of how they linked up. Call that the arrow's footprint.

Now ask any continuum law one question: *does it keep the footprint, or forget it?*

Some laws **keep** it. They are directional, structured, lumpy, phase-correlated. They still remember how their pieces got where they are. These are the laws Event Density builds directly, on the first rung. The law for how a wavefront advances. The law for how a shock forms when fast catches slow. The coherent electromagnetic field, the part of electromagnetism that carries structure. The law for how living things clump and aggregate. All of them keep the arrow's record, and all of them fall out of the substrate's direct coarse-graining, because the substrate is *made* of that record.

Other laws **forget** it. They are smooth, spread-out, isotropic, memoryless, Gaussian. Diffusion, the way heat and ink spread until every trace of where they started is gone. Viscosity, the way a fluid's swirls die into warmth. The bell-curve statistics of a settled field. And here is the thing worth slowing down for: going up a rung does not *un*-commit anything. It commits *while throwing away the record of how the commits were linked*. The single ingredient you always add to reach the second rung is **independence**, the assumption that neighbors have forgotten each other. That is not a technicality. That is forgetting, made into a step. Event Density reaches these laws, but only by taking that step, and only that step, and the theory names it in advance rather than smuggling it in.

## The sharpest version of the whole idea

If you want the thesis compressed into one line of chalk, it is this.

The wave equation and the heat equation, the law of remembering and the law of forgetting, are almost the same equation. They share the exact same spatial part, the same operator describing how a thing varies across space. The *only* difference between them is the time derivative. The wave equation uses a second-order one, and it propagates, reversibly, keeping its memory, ringing forever. The heat equation uses a first-order one, and it smooths, irreversibly, forgetting, settling to nothing.

So on that one shared operator, the difference between a law that remembers and a law that forgets is literally the order of the time derivative. And the order of the time derivative is the arrow. That is as formal as "remember versus forget" ever gets: two of the most important equations in physics, identical but for the one mark that Event Density puts at the foundation. When I say the arrow sorts the continuum, that is not a metaphor. It is the difference between a first derivative in time and a second one, and everything that difference decides.

## The coastline: where the theory says no

Now the part that makes me trust it, and the part this program insists on giving equal weight. There is a third bin, and it is the laws Event Density *cannot* build.

A soliton is a solitary wave that holds its exact shape forever, a perfect lump that never spreads, run-backward symmetric, a delicate balance preserved with no loss. It is a beautiful object, and it is real in the right medium. And it asks the substrate for something a thing made of commitment cannot give: to hold a structure perfectly *without committing*, to preserve rather than to advance. We tested this directly, not by argument but in simulation. Hand the bare substrate a soliton and it does not hold it. It disperses the packet. The lump spreads and dies.

That is a *measured negative*, and I want to be precise about what it is and is not. It is not a claim that solitons cannot exist, they plainly do. It is the claim that Event Density does not build one from the bottom rule alone, and the simulation says so. Add a higher layer, a focusing medium, and ED can host one. At the very bottom, it cannot, because the bottom is commitment, and a soliton needs commitment's absence. That is a wall, honestly found, and it is one of two. The other we met in the numbers: a finite-memory substrate provably cannot climb into the deep structure of the primes. One proven wall, one measured wall, both recorded with the same ink as the wins.

This is the whole reason the picture is trustworthy. A theory that reproduces every law you show it has told you nothing, because it would have reproduced any law at all. A theory that reproduces the laws that share its character, reaches the ones that forget it by a named and tested step, and *fails, visibly, on the ones that need its absence*, has drawn a coastline. It has said where the land ends. The arrow draws the coastline as well as the land.

## And gravity was this all along

One more thread, because it closes a loop back to the black-hole part. Event Density's rule for gravity, the one that in the weak-field limit reproduces the bending of light and the flat rotation of galaxies, turns out to *be* this two-layer structure, not merely to obey it. The rule has a smoothing term, a layer-two piece that spreads the gravitational field out, the same kind of forgetting that gives you diffusion. And it has a concentration term, a layer-one piece that pulls commitment together, the same kind of structure-building that gives you shocks and aggregation. Gravity is those two competing. Where the smoothing wins you get gentle curved space. Where the concentration wins, runs away, and pulls commitment into a pileup, you get a horizon, a black hole. The continuum-law map of this part and the black-hole map of part eight are not two results. They are one commitment, seen from two sides. It really does all fit, and it fits because it is all one primitive.

## What this is, and what it is not

Let me be square, since a unifying claim is exactly where a person should get suspicious.

This is not a claim that Event Density reaches every law of physics. The opposite is the result: the edges, the laws it cannot cast, are as much the point as the ones it can, and one of them was nailed down by a test that *failed on purpose*. It is not a solution to the hard mathematics behind fluids or gauge fields. Where those touch the famous Clay problems, Event Density supplies physical mechanism and explicitly disclaims the rigorous proofs, which are a different discipline. And the sort is held to a discipline so it cannot cheat: the ingredient added at each step is specified in advance and justified on its own, never tuned to land on the law you wanted, and "reached" is tested by whether the *constants* come out right, a validated diffusion coefficient, not merely whether the shape looks familiar. The diffusive results pass that coefficient test. The geometric ones are inherited as simulated. The soliton wall is measured. Each is tiered as exactly what it is.

What it *is*, is the closest thing this program has to a single picture of everything. One primitive, the arrow of time laid at the foundation, sorts the entire continuum: builds the laws that remember, reaches the laws that forget by the one honest act of forgetting, and is barred, visibly and by test, from the laws that need it gone. Heat and light and fluids and gravity and the primes all take their places in one map, and the map has a coastline the theory drew itself.

So here is where we land. Everything in this series has been the arrow, wearing a different face in each part. This part is the mirror that shows it is one face. The universe's continuum laws are not a random zoo; they are sorted, cleanly, by whether they keep or forget the record that commitment writes, and the sorting is as sharp as the difference between a first and a second derivative in time. A thing that commits builds a certain kind of world, reaches another kind by forgetting, and cannot build a third. That is not everything. But it is one reason, underneath a great many facts, and it knows its own edges.
