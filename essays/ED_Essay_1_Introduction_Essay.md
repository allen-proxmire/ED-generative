# The Arrow First
### An Introduction to Event Density

*Allen Proxmire*

---

Physics rests on a few choices so old we have stopped seeing them as choices. Here is one. The fundamental laws are written to run equally well forward and backward in time. Newton's equations do not care which way the clock turns. Neither do Maxwell's, or Schrödinger's, or Einstein's. Run the film of two billiard balls colliding in reverse and you see another perfectly legal collision. At the level of the basic equations, the future and the past are interchangeable.

And yet the one thing every living person knows, in their body, every waking second, is that time runs one way. Coffee cools. Glasses shatter and never reassemble. We remember yesterday and not tomorrow. The arrow of time is the most obvious fact about the world, and it appears nowhere in the foundational equations of physics. It is added afterward, quietly, as a statistical story about how disorder tends to grow. The arrow, in standard physics, is an afterthought.

Event Density begins by refusing that arrangement. It asks a simple question. What if the arrow is not an afterthought but the starting material? What if the one-way act of *something happening, and then being settled*, is the most basic thing there is, and the smooth reversible equations are the approximation we reach later, after we squint?

That is the whole move. Everything else follows from taking it seriously.

## The substrate

Most physics starts with stuff in space: particles, fields, a stage they move on. Event Density starts somewhere stranger and, I would argue, more honest. It starts with **relations**.

The intuition is old and, once you sit with it, hard to shake. A single thing, utterly alone, with nothing to relate to, is indistinguishable from nothing at all. It has no position, because position is a relation to other things. It has no motion, no size, no properties. To exist, in any sense we can talk about, is to participate in a web of relationships. Two is more fundamental than one. The relation comes before the things it relates.

So the foundation of Event Density is not a space full of objects. It is a **participation graph**: a vast web of relationships, with the things we call objects emerging as stable patterns *in* the web rather than residents *on* a stage. Space itself is not the floor. It is one of the patterns.

On this web, a few basic moves are allowed. Participation flows along **channels**, which are just directions a pattern can keep going in. Each channel carries a kind of phase, a **polarity**, the way a wave carries a phase. And there is a fixed budget, a **bandwidth**, so that not everything can happen at once. These are the gears. They matter, and the papers spell them out. But one move stands above the rest, because it is the one that breaks the symmetry of time.

## Commitment

In Event Density, the world advances by **commitment**. A region of the web is unsettled, holding several possibilities at once, and then it *commits*: the unsettled becomes settled, the indeterminate becomes determinate, and, crucially, **it does not come back**. Commitment is one-way. What has been laid down stays laid down.

This is the arrow, made into a primitive. Not derived, not added by hand, not a statistical tendency. Built in at the bottom, as the basic act of the substrate. The universe, in this picture, is not a frozen block of reversible equations that we happen to experience as flowing. It is a process that genuinely flows, because flowing, committing, settling, is what it *is*.

If you have ever wondered why you cannot remember the future, this is the answer Event Density gives. You remember the past because the past is what has been committed. The future has not been laid down yet. The asymmetry you feel is not a trick of statistics. It is the grain of the wood.

## Why this changes things

It would be a curiosity and nothing more if it stopped there. The reason to care is that putting the arrow first **inverts the usual stack**, and the inversion turns out to be productive.

In standard physics, the reversible laws are fundamental and the arrow is emergent. In Event Density it is the other way around: the arrow is fundamental, and the smooth reversible laws are what you get when you blur the picture, the way the smooth flow of water is what you get when you stop tracking individual molecules. Reversibility is not the truth underneath. It is the approximation on top.

Once you read physics this way, a pattern appears. The laws that *build and keep structure*, the ones with a direction and a memory, are the ones the substrate produces naturally, because building and committing is its native act. The laws that *erase structure*, that smooth and average and forget, are the ones you only reach by adding the forgetting back in. The famous "fundamental" equations that look perfectly time-symmetric are, in this view, the ones that have quietly forgotten the arrow. They are downstream, not beneath.

That single organizing idea, *the divide is the arrow*, sorts a surprising amount of physics. And it suggests, paper by paper, that one primitive might be doing far more work than anyone budgeted for.

## What one primitive seems to buy

Here I have to be careful, because this is exactly where a hopeful theory turns into an overconfident one, and I would rather undersell.

Starting from the substrate and the arrow, a good deal of physics that is normally *assumed* turns out to *follow*, or to be *reframed*. The rules of quantum mechanics, the ones usually written down as postulates, come out as consequences: why probabilities go as the square, why states live in the space they do, why measurement looks the way it does. The Schrödinger equation appears as the natural drift of the substrate's phase. Spin, the strange property that takes two full turns to return a particle to itself, shows up as a signature of being *tethered to the web* rather than floating free. Gravity emerges without a force and without dark matter, as a shortage of the substrate's bandwidth where matter has committed heavily. The galaxy-rotation scale and the dark-energy scale, two separate mysteries usually patched with two separate invisible substances, fall out of a single cosmic boundary. Even the number of spatial dimensions, the bare fact that there are three, has a candidate explanation: three is the only count in which the arrow can *hold the order of what it committed*, because a knot stays tied in three dimensions and comes undone in four.

I want to be honest about the tiers there. Some of those results are solid and simulated. Some are clean derivations. Some are still the frontier, structural arguments with an open premise named in plain sight. The matter sector and the dimensions are the newest and the least settled, and the papers say so. Event Density is not a finished theory of everything handed down complete. It is a **program**, building outward from one idea, with the edges marked.

## Not a theory of everything. A bet that can lose.

There is a failure mode for projects like this, and it is worth naming because Event Density is built to avoid it. A theory that can be bent to fit *any* observation explains nothing, because nothing could ever prove it wrong. The universe of cranks is full of frameworks that "explain everything" and therefore forbid nothing.

Event Density is built to be killable. It diverges from the standard picture on purpose, and divergence is a thing that can be checked. It says there is no dark-matter particle to find. It says gravity carries a faint extra signature that pure General Relativity does not. It says certain structures the math elsewhere permits will not be found stable in nature. It predicts a specific scale at which a specific quantum experiment should stop behaving quantum. Each of those is a place the theory has stuck its neck out. Each is a place it could lose its head.

That, more than any single derivation, is what I would ask a skeptical reader to weigh. Not whether Event Density explains a lot. Plenty of wrong ideas explain a lot. Whether it *risks* a lot. It does. The day one of those risky predictions is checked and fails, the theory is wrong, and I will say so. The day one is checked and holds, it will be something no amount of consilience could buy.

## Where this goes

What follows, across the papers and the videos in this series, is the build. From the substrate and the arrow, outward: quantum mechanics, then spin and antimatter, then the matter sector and where matter itself came from, then gravity and the dark sector, then black holes and the fate of information, then the early universe, and finally the lens that ties it together and the list of ways the whole thing could be wrong.

It is a lot to claim from one idea. I know that. I came to it not by wanting a grand theory but by not believing a few of the standard stories, the invisible matter, the landscape of unfalsifiable universes, and by wanting to understand where the matter in the world actually came from. Event Density is what grew out of those small refusals. Whether it is right is not for me to decide. That is what the predictions are for.

But I will say this much for the starting point. The arrow of time was always the most obvious thing in the room. It is strange that we built a physics that left it out. This is what happens if you put it back first.
