# Turn It Twice
### Spin, Antimatter, and the Dirac Equation from the Substrate

*Allen Proxmire*

*Series: Event Density, Part 3. Companion papers: `Paper_103_Cl31_FrameUniqueness`, `Paper_106_DiracEquation`, `Paper_102_SpinStatistics`, `Paper_112_hbar_Origin`.*

Take an electron. Turn it all the way around, a full three hundred and sixty degrees, back to where it started. It is not the same. To genuinely return it to its original state you have to turn it around again, twice in total, seven hundred and twenty degrees. Half a turn of the universe is not enough for an electron to come home.

This is not a metaphor and it is not a quirk of notation. It is measured, it is real, and it is one of the strangest facts in all of physics. Particles like the electron, the things you and everything you love are made of, do not come back to themselves after one full rotation. They are spinors, and a spinor lives on a world where a circle is twice as long as it looks.

There is a second strange fact, just as deep. Every matter particle comes with a mirror. For the electron there is the positron, identical in mass, opposite in charge, an anti-electron. Paul Dirac did not go looking for antimatter. In 1928 he was only trying to write down an equation for the electron that obeyed both quantum mechanics and special relativity at once. The equation he found, now carved on his memorial in Westminster Abbey, simply *had* extra solutions, and those solutions described a particle that should not exist. Four years later it was found in a cloud chamber. The mathematics predicted a mirror world before anyone had seen it.

Physics treats both of these, the double turn and the mirror, as facts that fall out of the Dirac equation, and it treats the Dirac equation itself as a kind of inspired guess. Dirac wanted his equation to be first-order and relativistic, so he needed strange objects that square to give space and time, and out of those objects fell spin, and antimatter, and the rest. Brilliant, but unexplained. Why those objects? Why the double turn? Why the mirror? The equation gives the *what*. It does not give the *why*.

Event Density gives the why, and all three come from one source: a particle is not a free point. It is a knot in a web, tethered, and dragged through by the arrow of time.

## The frame at a point: where the strange objects come from

Start with Dirac's strange objects, the ones that square to give spacetime. In the textbooks they are the gamma matrices, introduced as whatever is needed to make the equation work. They feel pulled out of a hat.

In the substrate they are not pulled out of anything. At any point in the participation graph there is a *local frame*, a way the three directions of space and the one direction of the arrow fit together right there. And the algebra of how those four directions compose, how you combine a step this way with a step that way, is a specific mathematical object called a **Clifford algebra**, the one written Cl(3,1), three space and one time. The gamma matrices are not a trick. They are the multiplication table of the substrate's own local frame.

The papers show something sharper still: that this frame is essentially *forced*. Given what the substrate is, three spatial directions and the one-way arrow, the Clifford structure that lives at each point is the unique one that fits. Dirac reached for these objects because they worked. Event Density says he was reaching for the substrate's frame without knowing it had a name.

## The double turn: spin as the mark of being tethered

Now the seven hundred and twenty degrees. Why does an electron need two full turns?

Here is the picture that makes it ordinary, and it is the deepest single idea in this part of the program. An electron is not a lonely dot sitting in empty space. It is a pattern *in* the web, connected to everything around it by its channels, its threads of participation. It is tethered.

Watch what happens when you rotate something that is tethered. Hold a coffee cup flat on your palm and rotate it under your arm without spilling it. After one full turn your arm is twisted into a knot. You cannot undo the twist. But keep going, rotate it a second full turn the same way, and your arm comes smoothly untwisted. Two turns, not one, to return everything, the cup and the threads, to the start. This is a real and famous demonstration, sometimes done with a belt or a plate, and it is exactly, mathematically, the behavior of a spinor.

A *free* point would not do this. A lonely dot can turn once and be done. But a tethered thing carries the memory of the turn in its threads, and that memory only clears after a second turn. **Spin one-half, the double turn, is the signature of being embedded in the web rather than floating free.** It is what relationality looks like when you rotate it. The most baffling property of matter, the thing that makes an electron need two turns to come home, is the substrate quietly telling you that nothing is ever truly alone. Two is more fundamental than one, read off a spinning frame.

I want to be honest that this is the newest piece in this video, a structural account rather than a closed proof, and the papers flag it as such. But it is the kind of reason the bare equation never offers.

## The mirror: where antimatter comes from

The third strange fact is the mirror, the antiparticle.

The arrow gives the substrate a direction, a sense of which way becoming runs. A particle is a participation-structure carried *along* that direction. And here is the thing: you can build the very same structure carried *against* it, the same pattern with its relationship to the arrow reversed. That reversed structure is the **antiparticle**. Antimatter is matter read against the grain of time.

I should be straight about the tier, because unlike the local frame and the double turn above, this one is not closed by the companion papers of this part. The antiparticle-from-the-arrow reading is an account, and its real development belongs to part four, where the arrow's first commitment does the work. Take it here as a preview of that, not a result I am leaning on the Dirac papers to have proven.

This is why antimatter is not some separate exotic substance that had to be added to the theory. It is the same matter, mirrored in the one mirror the substrate actually has, the arrow. The four parts of Dirac's spinor are exactly this: two for the particle, spin up and spin down, and two for its mirror, the antiparticle, spin up and spin down. Dirac found four components because the substrate offers four: a thing and its time-mirror, each able to spin two ways.

And this connects forward, to a question we will take up in part four. If matter and antimatter are the same structure read with opposite relation to the arrow, then the fact that the universe is full of matter and almost empty of antimatter is a fact about the arrow's *first* act of commitment. The mirror and the missing antimatter are the same story. We will get there.

## Exclusion: why two electrons will not share

One more, because it is too elegant to skip. Two electrons will not occupy the same state. This is the Pauli exclusion principle, and it is the reason atoms have structure, the reason chemistry exists, the reason matter is solid and does not collapse into a point. Without it there is no periodic table and no you.

In standard physics this is tied to spin by the spin-statistics theorem, a deep result that is true but famously hard to feel. In the substrate it has the same root as the double turn. Swapping two identical particles is a kind of rotation, a braid of their threads around each other. And a thing that picks up a twist after one turn picks up a *minus sign* when you swap it. That minus sign is exactly what forbids two of them from sharing a state, because a state and its own negative can only both be true if the state is nothing at all. So the solidity of the floor under your feet and the seven-hundred-and-twenty-degree turn of an electron are the same fact, the tether's twist, seen two different ways. One honesty check: this substrate picture still leans on the standard exchange bookkeeping of quantum field theory to carry the swap-twist the last step into the exclusion rule, so it is a conditional account, not a from-scratch derivation. With that machinery borrowed, the tether's minus sign is the substrate's fingerprint on why the rule holds. The structure of every atom traces back to the same source as spin.

## ħ: the size of a single becoming

Threaded through all of this is Planck's constant, ħ, the number that sets the scale of the quantum world. In standard physics it is a measured input, a brute fundamental constant.

In the substrate it has a meaning. It is the size of one quantum of *action*, the grain of a single act of becoming. The substrate does not commit continuously and smoothly, it commits in discrete events, and ħ is the size of one. This is the same idea we met in part two, where ħ measured how long phase survives between commitments, the substrate's coherence scale. Here it wears its other face: not a duration but a *unit*, the smallest indivisible parcel of happening. Either way, ħ stops being a number the universe simply has, and becomes a number the substrate *is*, the size of its fundamental step.

## One source, three mysteries

Look at what we have. The strange objects in Dirac's equation are the substrate's local frame. The double turn is the mark of being tethered to the web. The antiparticle is matter mirrored in the arrow. Exclusion is the tether's twist under a swap. And ħ is the size of one act of becoming. Five things that physics carries as separate brute facts, and under the substrate they are one thing wearing five faces: a participation-structure, embedded in the web, dragged through by the arrow, in steps of size ħ.

The Dirac equation, which the textbooks present as a lucky guess that happened to predict a mirror universe, becomes in this light the most natural statement you could write: how a tethered, phase-carrying knot moves through the substrate's frame. Spin, antimatter, and exclusion were never separate gifts. They were the same gift, the tether, opened three times.

## What this is, honestly

Same caution as before, because it keeps me honest and keeps your trust. None of this changes a prediction of the Dirac theory. Event Density reproduces spin, antimatter, and exclusion, it does not revise them. If you came for an experiment that comes out differently, that is later in the series, in the matter sector and in gravity. What this part offers is the *reason*: why the electron must be turned twice, why it comes with a mirror, why two will not share a seat. Those reasons were always missing, treated as the price of admission to particle physics. The substrate hands them back.

There is still one way this part can be wrong, and it is worth naming. If the Dirac equation really is the substrate's own frame in motion, then a fundamental spin-one-half particle must obey the Dirac equation and no other first-order law, because the Cl(3,1) frame is the one the substrate forces. Find a fundamental spin-one-half particle whose propagation does not follow Dirac, or a rival first-order law in that same frame that nature actually uses, and the claim that the equation is the substrate showing through, rather than a lucky guess, is broken. The companion paper states this falsifier plainly, and I will too.

And the newest of them, spin from being tethered, I have marked as a structural account, not a finished derivation, because that is what it is. I would rather you know exactly which floorboards are solid and which are still being laid. But even as an account, it does something the equation alone never did. It tells you that the strangeness of matter is not strangeness at all. It is connection. An electron has to turn twice for the same reason a story needs two characters before anything can happen. Nothing in this universe stands alone, and spin is the proof, written into every atom in your body.
