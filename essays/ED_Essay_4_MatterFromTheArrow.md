# The One in a Billion
### Baryogenesis, the Forces, and Why the Universe Is Left-Handed

*Allen Proxmire*

*Series: Event Density, Part 4. Companion papers: `Paper_ED_Baryogenesis`, `Paper_MS-II_MatterSectorFromTheArrow`.*

---

Look at anything. Your hand, a star, the screen in front of you. It is made of matter, and not antimatter, and that simple fact is one of the deepest unsolved mysteries in physics.

The standard story is this: in the first instants after the Big Bang, the universe made matter and antimatter in almost perfectly equal amounts. They are mirror twins, and when a particle meets its anti-twin they annihilate completely, both gone in a flash of light. If the amounts had been exactly equal, that is how the story would have ended: a universe of light, and nothing else. No stars, no planets, no you.

But the amounts were not exactly equal. For unknown reasons, for every billion antimatter particles, there were about a billion and one matter particles. The billion pairs found each other and vanished. The single leftover did not. Everything you have ever seen, every atom in your body and in every star in the sky, is built from that one-in-a-billion survivor. We are the rounding error in a near-perfect cancellation.

And so the question physics has been stuck on for 60 years is why there was an asymmetry. Why the imbalance? This is called baryogenesis. By the textbook physics, the leftover should have been far smaller than it is. The matter we are made of is, on paper, a number too big to explain.

Event Density has a different answer, and it is almost embarrassingly direct. The early universe was too saturated to allow two patterns to form, and it had to pick left or right. 

## The arrow had to choose

Recall what the antiparticle was, from part three. A particle is a participation-structure carried along the arrow of time. Its antiparticle is the same structure carried against the arrow. Matter and antimatter are not two different substances. They are one structure, read two ways, with the only mirror the substrate actually has: the direction of becoming.

Now think about the very first commitment. The universe begins, and the substrate settles for the first time, lays down its first irreversible act. That act has a direction, because the arrow is one-way, that is the whole point of it. And to be carried *along* a direction is precisely what makes something matter rather than antimatter. So the universe's first commitment does not get to be neutral. The act of committing, of settling in a definite direction, is itself the choice of matter over its mirror.

The imbalance is not a fine-tuned accident that the theory has to explain. It is the arrow being an arrow. A universe that commits at all is a universe that has picked a side, and the side it picks is the matter we are made of. You do not need Sakharov's special conditions stacked up just so. You need the substrate to do the one thing it always does, settle in a direction, and the asymmetry is already there.

I want to be honest about the tier of this. It is what the papers call an *account*, a coherent and ED-native explanation, not a closed derivation with the final number computed. There are pieces still open, and I will name the most important one in a moment. But even as an account, it reframes the mystery. Baryogenesis stops being "why is there a strange leftover" and becomes "of course there is a leftover, because the universe is the kind of thing that commits."

## The same choice made the universe left-handed

Here is where it gets, I think, genuinely beautiful, and it is the newest result in this part of the program.

There is a second great asymmetry in nature, seemingly unrelated. The weak nuclear force, the one behind radioactive decay, is *left-handed*. It couples to particles spinning one way and ignores particles spinning the other. This is called parity violation, and when it was discovered in 1956 it stunned physicists, because there had been no reason to expect the universe to care about left versus right at all. Like baryogenesis, it is usually treated as a separate brute fact: the weak force just is lopsided, nobody quite knows why.

Event Density says these two mysteries are one.

A commitment is a handed event. It carries a phase, and it carries a *twist*, a screw sense, the same screw that gave us spin in part three. The phase sets the matter-versus-antimatter reference. The twist sets the left-versus-right reference. And the universe's first commitment imprints *both at once*, in the same act. The choice that made matter outweigh antimatter and the choice that made the weak force left-handed are not two coincidences. They are two faces of a single event, the arrow's first settling, seen from two angles.

Both choices are also *maximal*, all-or-nothing, and for the same reason: they are topological, the kind of thing that cannot be a little bit one way. That matches reality, where parity violation is total and the matter excess, while tiny in proportion, is utterly one-sided in sign. Two of the largest unexplained asymmetries in physics collapse into a single sentence: *the arrow's first commitment chose matter over antimatter and left over right, in one stroke, because it could not choose neither.*

The honest open edge, the piece I promised to name, is *why the weak force specifically* inherits the handedness, rather than one of the other forces. Event Density has a candidate answer, that the weak force is the one coupled directly to commitment, but that is the least-settled step in the whole account, and the papers flag it as the frontier. So take the unification as a powerful reframing with one bolt still loose, not as a finished proof. That is exactly the tier it is.

## Now, the forces themselves

We have been talking about matter. Where do the forces that act on it come from? Here the substrate is more grounded, and one piece is a clean derivation.

Recall channels, the directions a pattern can keep going in. A force, in Event Density, is what happens when there are *several* indistinguishable channels of the same kind running together, and the substrate has to keep track of how they mix while conserving its bandwidth. The mathematics of that bookkeeping is exactly the mathematics of a *gauge group*, the structures physicists use to describe forces. One channel gives the simplest force, electromagnetism. Two channels mixing gives the weak force's structure. Three gives the strong force's. The forces are not a separate ingredient added to the matter. They are the substrate keeping its accounts when channels run in parallel.

This also quietly defeats the oldest objection to any theory like this. People say the universe cannot be built from a discrete web, because a famous theorem shows that discrete worlds come out mirror-symmetric, unable to have a left-handed weak force at all. But that theorem needs two specific assumptions, a rigid repeating lattice and a time-symmetric rule, and Event Density has neither. Its web is relational, not a rigid grid, and its rule is the one-way arrow. The objection's own premises are the two things the substrate refuses to have. The door it tries to slam was never on this house.

And the spin we met in part three sits right here too. The matter that feels these forces is tethered to the web, which is why it must be turned twice to come home, and which is why it obeys exclusion and builds atoms. Forces from channels, spin from the tether, chirality from the screw, matter from the arrow: the whole matter sector is the substrate, read in different directions.

## What it bets, and what it does not

Two honest notes, because they are the whole reason to trust the rest.

This part of the program leans heavily on accounts and structural readings rather than closed proofs, more so than the quantum mechanics of part two, and I have tried to mark each tier as it came. The gauge-groups-from-channels step is a genuine derivation. The spin-from-tethering and the chirality story are structural. The baryogenesis-and-parity unification is an account with one open bolt. The matter sector is the frontier of the whole program, and I would rather hand you the map with the unfinished roads drawn in than sell you a finished country.

But it does stick its neck out. It says there is *no* parity-violating version of electromagnetism to be found, because the abelian force is the handedness-blind one, and general physics permits such a force even though ED leaves no room for it. That is a real prediction, checkable, able to be wrong. And in the next part, the count of the forces themselves, the bare fact that there are three and not seventeen, will reduce to a single number, with the prediction that no stable larger force can exist. The matter sector does not just explain. It forbids, in the careful sense, it tells you what you will not find.

The mystery we started with was why there is anything at all, why the great cancellation left a remainder. The answer Event Density gives is that a universe which commits is a universe that takes a side, and taking a side is what leaves matter standing and makes the weak force lean left, in one and the same first act. We are the one in a billion. And the reason we are here, rather than a universe of light and nothing, is that the arrow, at the very beginning, could not point both ways.
