Here is the most uncomfortable fact in modern cosmology, and physicists will tell it to you plainly if you ask. We can account for about five percent of the universe. The atoms in stars, planets, gas, dust, you, everything we have ever directly seen, that is five percent. The other ninety-five is split between two things we have named but cannot explain. About a quarter is *dark matter*, an unseen mass that holds galaxies together, because the galaxies we see are spinning far too fast to hold themselves. And about seventy percent is *dark energy*, a mysterious pressure pushing the universe apart faster and faster. Two unknown substances, no confirmed detection of either, making up almost the entire contents of reality. That is the standard picture, and it works, and it is also an enormous IOU.

Event Density looks at that ninety-five percent and says something that sounds reckless until you see how it lands: there is no dark stuff. Not dark matter the particle, not dark energy the substance. There is one field, the same field that does ordinary gravity, and the two great mysteries are that one field read at the scale of the whole cosmos. One boundary, not two ghosts. This is the part of the series where the theory stops merely re-explaining known physics and starts sticking its neck out far enough to be cut off. So let me be careful, and let me show you exactly where the neck is.

## Gravity, from the field of connection

Start with ordinary gravity, because the dark sectors are just its far ends.

The substrate's basic currency is what earlier parts called bandwidth: how much capacity a region has for connection, for committing new relationships. Call it *b*. Where matter piles up, it draws down that capacity, and *b* falls. And two things happen together when *b* falls. Space curves, and clocks slow. That is gravity. Not a force reaching across a void, but the local depletion of the substrate's capacity, felt as curved space and slow time.

The crucial word is *together*. In Event Density the very same field sets how space bends and how fast clocks run; they are locked, because they are two readings of one *b*. And that locking is not a footnote. It is the single most important test in the history of gravity. When light from a distant star grazes the Sun, it bends. A theory where gravity only slows clocks predicts a certain deflection. Einstein's theory, where gravity curves space *and* slows clocks, predicts exactly twice that. In 1919 they measured it, got the factor of two, and that is the morning Einstein became famous. It is the result that killed the simpler scalar theories of gravity stone dead.

Event Density gets the factor of two, and for the deepest possible reason: one field cannot help but curve space and time at once, so the two contributions are forced to be equal and they add. The papers ray-trace it in simulation and land on 2.09 against the predicted 2.00, with a control case rigged to have no bending coming out at exactly zero. So the substrate reproduces the weak-field Einstein gravity and passes its classical tests. That part is solid, derived and simulated.

## Einstein, plus the arrow, and nothing else

What *kind* of gravity is this, exactly? There is a theorem saying that once you ask for a sensible gravity in our world, you are forced toward Einstein's, unless one extra field comes along for the ride. In Event Density, one does, and its identity is the whole signature of the theory. Because the arrow of time is built into the law rather than added on top, the substrate carries a preferred sense of "now," and that shows up as a single extra ingredient riding alongside Einstein's gravity. The papers call it the khronon, the arrow itself made into a field. So Event Density's gravity is *Einstein plus the arrow, and nothing else*.

That sounds dangerous, because theories with a preferred "now" usually die fast against precision tests. This one has an alibi, and it is worth one honest sentence. The same unity that creates the extra field also protects it: matter and geometry are one substrate sharing one causal cone, so gravitational waves travel at exactly light speed as an identity, not a tuning, which is the constraint that wiped out a generation of rival theories after 2017. And the preferred-frame effects that usually betray such theories are, in the companion paper, computed and found either exactly zero or suppressed by an enormous margin, because the substrate commits its events sparsely. I will not belabor the machinery. The point is that the one deviation Event Density makes from textbook gravity, the arrow, is located, accounted for, and so far survives.

Now the two far ends.

## Dark matter: the field's deep end

A spiral galaxy spins. By Newton and the visible mass, the stars at the outer edge should orbit slowly, the way Neptune orbits slower than Earth. They do not. The outer stars move just as fast as the inner ones, far too fast to be held by the gravity of the matter we can see. They should have flung themselves into the dark long ago. The standard repair is to pour in invisible mass, dark matter, until the books balance.

Event Density repairs it differently. At the very low accelerations found in the outskirts of a galaxy, the bandwidth field's response changes character. Gravity does not stay strictly Newtonian; it gets a boost, of exactly the form that has been known for forty years to fit galaxy rotation without any dark matter. The boosted law is not bolted on. It is the deep, low-acceleration regime of the same field that does ordinary gravity, the khronon read where its background, cosmic time, dominates.

And here is the bet, the parameter-free one, the reason this chapter matters. That boost switches on at a particular acceleration, a threshold the galaxy data pins at about a ten-billionth of a meter per second squared. In the standard story that threshold is just a number you fit. In Event Density it is *not free*. It is the speed of light times the cosmic expansion rate, divided by two-pi. The cosmic expansion rate sets an edge to the observable universe, a boundary at distance *c* divided by *H*-naught, and the threshold is that boundary's fingerprint on a local accelerating object, with the two-pi coming from the geometry of one full turn, the same kind of two-pi we met at the black-hole horizon. Put the measured cosmic rate in, and the predicted threshold comes out within about ten percent of the measured one, with *zero free parameters*.

That is the wedge, and it is a real one. The scale where galaxies stop obeying Newton is tied, by this theory, to the size of the universe. People have noticed that numerical coincidence for decades and shrugged. Event Density says it is not a coincidence, it is a mechanism, and stakes itself on the match. If sharper measurements of the cosmic rate push the predicted threshold off the measured one, or if isolated wide pairs of stars at exactly these accelerations turn out to obey plain Newton after all, this dies. Dark matter, in this picture, is not a particle anyone will ever catch. It is the deep end of the field that makes time one-way.

## Dark energy: the same edge, read again

Now the larger piece, the seventy percent. The expansion of the universe is speeding up, and the standard account assigns that to a vacuum energy, a constant pressure built into empty space, called Lambda. It is famous for being the worst quantitative prediction in physics: the naive estimate of its value is too large by a hundred and twenty orders of magnitude. A one followed by a hundred and twenty zeroes, wrong.

Event Density's claim here is more modest than its dark-matter claim, and I want to be scrupulous about that, because this is exactly where it would be tempting to oversell. What the theory says is that the dark-energy scale lives on the *same cosmic boundary* as the galaxy threshold, the same edge at *c* over *H*-naught. And the notorious hundred-and-twenty-orders smallness stops being a separate miracle. It becomes just the ratio of two scales the theory already has, the cosmic rate against the Planck rate, squared. Written that way, the "impossible" smallness is simply that the universe is very large and very old compared to the Planck scale, which we already knew.

But here is the honest line, and it is the most important sentence in this section. Event Density *identifies the form* and *locates the scale on the boundary*; it does not yet *derive the number from the bottom up*. The papers are blunt about this: the first naive attempt to compute the value from substrate parameters missed by sixty orders of magnitude and is kept on the record as a failure. The honest version reduces the whole question to one still-open derivation, getting the cosmic rate itself out of the substrate, and until that closes, the value of dark energy is *inherited from observation, not predicted*. So take this as a reframing, a strong one, the miracle turned into a ratio and parked on the same edge as everything else cosmic, but not as a solved number. The dark-matter scale is a parameter-free hit. The dark-energy value is a promissory note with the math half-done. Different tiers, and I would rather you hold them apart.

## The flex: one edge, two scales, no substances

Step back and see the shape. Two numbers that have no business being related, the acceleration where galaxies go strange and the energy density of empty space, are carried here by *one field* anchored to *one boundary*, the edge of the observable universe at *c* over *H*-naught. In the standard picture these are two unrelated mysteries needing two unknown substances, and their stubborn numerical echoes of the cosmic scale are just accidents nobody is allowed to ask about. Here they are the same boundary wearing two hats, because the field has only one scale to offer.

And the field was not invented for either job. It was *counted* into the theory as the arrow of time, forced to exist by an argument about irreversibility, long before dark matter or dark energy were anywhere in the picture. The theory does not buy a new substance to patch each hole. It spends a field it already owned, the arrow, and finds that the same field, read at the scale of the cosmos, is what we had been calling the dark.

## What this is, and what it is not

The careful accounting, because this is the chapter most worth being careful in.

This does not overturn Einstein. It reproduces the weak-field gravity and its classical tests, the factor of two included. It does not *solve* the dark sectors in one stroke. The galaxy threshold is a genuine parameter-free prediction that matches to about ten percent, and that is the strong card. But the full cosmological job, getting clusters and the early-universe afterglow exactly right, is addressable in the framework, not yet discharged. And the dark-energy value, as I said plainly, is inherited and not derived. Two scales, the Planck scale and the cosmic rate, are taken from the world as inputs, not produced from nothing.

What makes this worth your attention is that it *diverges*, loudly, where it could simply have hedged. It predicts there is no dark-matter particle to find, so every future direct-detection result is a chance to break it. It ties the galaxy threshold to the size of the universe, so a sharper cosmic rate is a chance to break it. It says isolated wide binaries at the right accelerations must depart from Newton, checkable now. A theory that explains everything and forbids nothing is a theory that can never be wrong, which is the same as never being science. This one tells you, in three separate places, the observation that would end it. That is not a weakness in the chapter. It is the reason to take the chapter seriously.

So here is where it leaves us. The ninety-five percent of the universe we called dark was never a pile of hidden stuff. We were hunting for substances when we should have been looking at the arrow, and at the one number that sets the scale of everything, the edge of what we can see. The dark matter is the field of time read in the shallows of a galaxy. The dark energy is the same field read at the rim of the cosmos. Not two ghosts haunting the five percent we understand. One field, one edge, and a theory honest enough to tell you how to prove it wrong.
