# What the Black Hole Remembers
### Hawking Radiation, the Page Curve, and Why the Information Paradox Never Quite Forms

*Allen Proxmire*

*Series: Event Density, Part 8. Companion papers: `Paper_039_HorizonDecoupling`, `Paper_047_HawkingSpectrum`, `Paper_050_PageCurve`, `Paper_052_BH_ParadoxSynthesis`, `BH_Thermal2Pi_FromNearHorizonRindler`.*

Drop a book into a black hole. Not metaphorically, an actual book, with actual words in it. The hole swallows it, and for a while nothing seems wrong. But black holes are not eternal. Stephen Hawking showed in 1974 that they glow, faintly, and that the glow carries energy away, so that over an unimaginable span of time the hole shrinks and finally evaporates. And here is the problem that has haunted physics ever since. Hawking's glow appeared to be *featureless*. Pure heat, the same dull static no matter what fell in, a phone book or a love letter or a dying star. If the hole evaporates completely and the radiation it leaves behind carries no trace of what went in, then the book is gone. Not burned, not scattered. Erased. Deleted from reality.

And quantum mechanics says that cannot happen. One of its deepest rules is that information is never truly destroyed. You can scramble a book past all hope of reading it, but in principle the past is always recoverable from the present. The universe keeps its receipts. A black hole that erases a book breaks that rule, and a black hole that obeys it breaks Hawking's calculation. This is the black-hole information paradox, and for fifty years it has been the sharpest place where our two best theories, gravity and the quantum, openly contradict each other.

Event Density has a way of looking at this where the paradox never gets off the ground. The short version is that the black hole *does* remember, just slowly and imperfectly, and an imperfect forgetting is only a slow telling. Let me walk it.

## A paradox standing on three legs

The contradiction is built from three claims, and you can see it has to break if you lay them side by side.

One, the radiation is featureless, pure heat, carrying nothing about what fell in. Two, the hole evaporates all the way, vanishing completely. Three, information is never destroyed. Hold all three and you have nonsense: the stuff that went in is gone, the radiation never told you anything, and yet information cannot be lost. Standard approaches accept all three legs as nearly untouchable and then perform delicate surgery to escape, walls of fire at the horizon, wormholes linking the inside to the outside, exotic replacements for the horizon itself. Clever, and maybe right in their own settings. Event Density does something blunter. It looks at the three legs and finds that two of them were never quite true to begin with, and the reason is the same reason both times.

## The horizon is a fog bank, not a wall

Start with the horizon, the surface of no return. In Einstein's picture it is a sharp geometric edge, a line in spacetime past which light itself cannot climb back out. In Event Density it is something softer and, I think, more honest about what it physically is. Recall from earlier in the series that the substrate carries cross-talk, correlations stitching one region to its neighbors. As matter piles up toward forming a black hole, that cross-talk between the inside and the outside *thins*, and at the horizon it fades to nothing. The horizon is not a cliff. It is a fog bank, the place where the connection between in and out goes statistically silent.

I want to be honest about a limit here, because the papers are. Event Density does not yet derive *where* that fog bank sits from its own first principles. It takes the horizon's location from Einstein's theory as a reference point and supplies the substrate mechanism that lives there. Deriving the geometry itself, the size of the hole from the substrate alone, is a separate program, named and not yet finished. So read this as: ED tells you *what the horizon is made of*, a fading of cross-talk, while still borrowing *where it is*. That is the tier.

But notice what a fog bank gives you that a wall does not. A wall is perfect. A fog is not. And that single difference is what unravels the paradox.

## The glow, and the rhythm underneath it

First, where does Hawking's glow even come from, in this picture? Near the fading surface, the substrate's correlations pick up a periodic rhythm, a recurring beat in imaginary time. It is a known and beautiful fact that a periodic beat like that *is* a temperature; a system that repeats on that schedule is indistinguishable from one sitting in a warm bath. So the black hole glows, at the Hawking temperature, for the same reason a struck bell has a pitch. The rate of that beat is set by the steepness of the horizon, and the conversion between the two runs through a factor of two-pi.

That two-pi is worth a sidebar, because we earned it elsewhere in this series. It is not a borrowed number. It is the angle of one full turn around the horizon in the substrate's own near-horizon geometry, which we showed lands exactly on the Rindler shape that forces the temperature to be the surface steepness divided by two-pi. So the glow's temperature is pinned by the substrate's geometry, not imported. Good.

## Leg one falls: the glow has fingerprints

Now the first crack. Is the glow truly featureless? Only if the substrate had a perfect, infinitely fine memory. It does not. It has a shortest grain, a finite memory, a smallest interval it can resolve. And a system with finite memory cannot produce perfectly featureless heat. The glow comes out *almost* thermal, with tiny deviations, fingerprints, scaling as the square of the radiation's frequency measured against the substrate's cutoff. These fingerprints are sign-definite and they grow as the hole shrinks, and crucially they are *information-bearing*. They are the channel through which the trace of what fell in rides back out. The radiation was never blank. It was heat with the faintest writing on it, writing you could only read if you could measure the spectrum finely enough.

This is the part of the chapter that is a genuine, killable prediction, not a re-telling. Event Density says the Hawking spectrum is *not exactly thermal*, and it says the precise shape and sign of the deviation. Real black holes glow far too faintly for us to check, but laboratory stand-ins for horizons, made in flowing fluids and cold-atom clouds, glow at frequencies near their own cutoff, where the fingerprint is large. Those analog experiments are the near-term test. If their radiation comes out exactly thermal, with no deviation of this form, this prediction is dead. That is the wedge, stated as something that can fail.

## Leg two falls: the hole leaves a remnant

The second crack comes from the same finite memory, which I find satisfying. A system with a shortest grain has a *highest note it can sing*. It cannot emit above its cutoff frequency. Now watch the hole shrink. As a black hole loses mass it gets hotter, and the typical frequency of its glow climbs. For an ordinary hole this is far below the cutoff and nothing stops it. But as the hole approaches the Planck mass, the tiniest mass scale in physics, the frequency it wants to emit rises right up to the substrate's ceiling. And there it stops. It cannot sing higher, so it cannot shed the last of its mass. Evaporation halts.

So the hole does *not* vanish. It leaves a stable remnant, frozen at about the Planck mass, a speck holding whatever has not yet been told. The papers get this cleanly: set the hole's emission frequency equal to the substrate's cutoff and the leftover mass falls out as exactly the Planck mass, at leading order, with only an order-one factor inherited. Leg two, complete evaporation, was false. The hole stops just short of nothing.

## The Page curve: watching the books balance

Put those together and you can track the actual bookkeeping over the hole's whole life, which is what the famous *Page curve* does. The quantity to watch is how entangled the outgoing radiation is with whatever remains inside. Early on it climbs: each quantum that leaves carries a thread back to the interior, and the threads pile up. If that were the whole story it would climb forever, and that endless climb is exactly the paradox. But the substrate has only a finite budget of threads it can hold across the fog bank. At a certain moment, the famous Page time, that budget saturates. After that, new threads cannot connect out to in, so they re-route, tying the late radiation to the *early* radiation instead. The entanglement with the interior stops rising and starts falling, back toward nothing. Up, then down. That arc, the Page curve, is the signature of information actually coming back out, and Event Density produces its shape from the finite budget and the re-routing.

Honesty about tier, because it matters here most. ED reproduces the *shape* of the Page curve, the rise and the turn and the fall, but it leans on two declared assumptions to do it: that the thread budget is finite, and that the threads re-route when it saturates. And the precise numbers, when the turn happens, how high it peaks, are taken from the standard result, not generated. So this is a substrate re-telling of a known curve, resting on stated postulates, not a from-scratch derivation of every number. I would rather you know exactly where the borrowed pieces are.

## Why the paradox never forms

Now stand back. The paradox needed featureless radiation *and* complete evaporation. Event Density has neither. The radiation carries fingerprints, so it was never blank. The hole leaves a remnant, so it never fully vanished. The books balance at every stage: information rides out in the deviations from pure heat, the entanglement turns over right on cue, and whatever is left sits in the remnant. There is no moment where something is destroyed, so there is nothing to rescue with firewalls or wormholes. The paradox is not solved so much as never permitted to assemble. Take away either false leg and the contradiction has nothing to stand on.

## What this is, and what it is not

Square accounting, because this chapter borrows more than some others and I want that visible.

This does not overturn Hawking and it does not overturn quantum mechanics. It reproduces the glow, the temperature, and the Page curve that decades of work established, and it tells that story in the substrate's own vocabulary, a fading of cross-talk, a finite memory, a thread budget. Several pieces are honest re-tellings resting on declared assumptions, and the horizon's location is still borrowed from Einstein. And the unitarity it preserves is unitarity *at the substrate level*, the substrate's own books balancing; tying that rigorously to the textbook quantum-mechanical statement in the black-hole setting is real work the papers explicitly leave open. No grand proof is claimed.

What *is* new, and what can kill it, are two specific bets that the same fact underwrites. The radiation is not exactly thermal, with a deviation of a stated size, sign, and shape, testable in laboratory horizons. And evaporation ends in a Planck-mass remnant rather than in nothing. Find exactly thermal analog radiation, or watch a hole evaporate cleanly to zero, and this picture breaks. That is the deal, and it is the right kind of deal.

So here is the through-line, because one fact does all of it. The substrate has a finite memory. That finiteness sets the highest note the hole can sing, which leaves the remnant. It blurs the glow away from perfect heat, which writes the fingerprints. It caps the thread budget, which bends the Page curve. A black hole is not a perfect eraser, because nothing built from a finite memory can erase perfectly. It forgets, but slowly, and imperfectly, and a forgetting that leaks is just the universe telling you, very quietly and over a very long time, what it swallowed.
