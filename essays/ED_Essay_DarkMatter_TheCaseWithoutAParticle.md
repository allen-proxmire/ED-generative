# The Case Without a Particle
### What the Bullet Cluster, a Galaxy With No Dark Matter, and One Stubborn Acceleration Are Really Telling Us

*Allen Proxmire*

*Series: Event Density. Companion papers: `Paper_029_a0`, `Paper_ED_DF2_DF4_GroupSuppression`, `Bullet_TopologicalDefect`.*

---

There is a version of Don Lincoln I have in mind for this one. Not the man exactly, but the honest position he once described. Twenty years ago, he said, if you had asked him whether the universe was full of invisible particles or whether we simply did not understand gravity, he would have shrugged toward the second. We do not understand gravity. Then two pieces of evidence changed his mind. The Bullet Cluster, where the gravity is plainly sitting in the wrong place, away from the ordinary matter. And later a galaxy, NGC 1052-DF2, that appears to have no dark matter at all. Those two together did it. If you can find a galaxy *without* the stuff, then the stuff is a thing, a substance, something a galaxy can have or lack. And if the gravity in the Bullet has walked away from the gas, then the stuff moved, like matter moves. Substance you can separate from ordinary matter. That is a particle. That is dark matter.

I want to take that reasoning seriously, because it is good reasoning. It is not the lazy version. It is the version that noticed the two observations which genuinely do cut against "we just don't understand gravity," and updated honestly. Most modified-gravity stories die exactly here, on DF2 and the Bullet, and they deserve to. So the question I want to put on the table is not "was Don wrong." It is narrower and fairer: is there a third option that was not on the table when he updated, one that survives both observations and, unlike a particle, actually predicts the one number nobody can explain?

Event Density is that third option, and it is not the old MOND that DF2 was built to kill. Let me lay out its reading of the three exhibits, and I will be honest at every step about what is solid and what is still a promissory note.

## The frame

Here is the whole disagreement in one sentence. The standard view says matter makes gravity: put the mass somewhere and the field faithfully reports it. Event Density says gravity is the coarse-grained shadow of the substrate's *organizational* state, the way temperature is the coarse-grained shadow of a lot of jostling molecules. Almost always, that organizational state tracks the matter, so the two views agree and you never catch them apart. The interesting cases are the ones where they come apart: where the substrate's structure gets displaced from the matter, or suppressed relative to it, or where it carries a fingerprint that no local lump of matter could carry. Those are exactly the three exhibits. Let me take the fingerprint first, because it is the one a particle cannot fake.

## Exhibit one: an acceleration that knows the size of the universe

Galaxies have a tell, and it has been sitting in plain sight for forty years. Below a certain acceleration, roughly a ten-billionth of a meter per second squared, the motion stops matching the visible matter and starts running fast, the flat rotation curves everyone has seen. That threshold has a name, *a-nought*, and here is the strange part. Its value is almost exactly the speed of light times the Hubble constant, the number that sets the expansion rate of the whole universe. Write it out and a-nought is about *c* times *H* -naught. The acceleration where individual galaxies start behaving strangely is tuned to the size of the cosmos.

Sit with how odd that is for the particle picture. Dark matter is supposed to be local clumps, halos of stuff wrapped around each galaxy. A clump of particles in a galaxy has no way to know the Hubble constant. There is no wire running from the edge of the observable universe to the outskirts of a spiral galaxy telling it where to switch behavior. In the particle account, the near-equality of a-nought and *cH*-naught is a coincidence with no fundamental status, a scale that just happens to land there. People have noticed the coincidence for decades and mostly set it aside, because nothing in the dark-matter framework has any use for it.

Event Density has a use for it. In this picture, every chain laying down its record is reading a faint anisotropy off the cosmic decoupling surface, the substrate's outer horizon at radius *c* over *H*-naught, the distance past which the expansion outruns the substrate's own signals. Project that far-away surface onto a locus that happens to be accelerating, keep the leading asymmetric piece, and the geometry of that projection hands you a clean factor. The acceleration scale comes out as *c* times *H*-naught, divided by two pi.

That two pi is the load-bearing part, and it is worth being exact about the tier. The number is geometric, the ordinary normalization you get whenever you project onto the lowest mode of a circle, the same two pi that shows up in every Fourier series. It is forced, not fit. The Hubble constant in the formula is not derived; it is measured cosmology, plugged in. So this is what the program calls form-forced and value-inherited: the substrate fixes the *shape* of the law and the relation between the scales, while the raw number *H*-naught is handed to it by observation. Put the measured Hubble constant in, and out comes about 1.08, against a measured a-nought of about 1.2, in the same units. Ten percent, with no free parameter to turn.

I am not going to oversell a ten percent match. It is not a proof of anything. But notice what kind of claim it is, because the kind matters more than the closeness. A particle theory can accommodate a-nought by adjusting halo profiles, but it cannot *predict* that a-nought should equal *cH*-naught over two pi, because in a particle theory there is no reason those two things should have anything to do with each other. Event Density says they must, and says exactly by what factor, and can be killed if the factor is wrong. If a sharper Hubble measurement drags the predicted a-nought outside the measured range, this piece is dead. That is the trade I want: a claim narrow enough to die, in exchange for explaining a coincidence the other side has to shrug off.

## Exhibit two: the galaxy with nothing in it

Now DF2, the observation that, more than any other, is supposed to prove dark matter is a substance. The logic is clean and I will state it at full strength. If gravity were simply modified at low accelerations, the modification would apply to every low-acceleration system, everywhere, always. It is a law, and laws do not take days off. So you should never find a galaxy that behaves purely by its visible matter with no boost at all. But DF2 is exactly that: a diffuse galaxy whose stars move as slowly as their own mass predicts, with no missing-mass signature. A galaxy that lacks the effect. And a law you can locally switch off is not a law. Therefore the effect is not a law of gravity, it is a substance, and this galaxy is one that happens not to have much of it. That is the argument, and it is a genuinely good one against plain MOND.

Here is why it does not land the same blow on Event Density. In this framework the boost is not an unconditional law of motion. It is the substrate spending *slack*, its uncommitted outer-scale capacity, and slack is a finite thing that can be used up. An isolated galaxy sitting alone in a void has all its outer-scale capacity free at its own location, so the boost appears, and you read it as missing mass. But DF2 is not isolated. It sits inside the NGC 1052 group, in the gravitational field of a big neighbor. Run the numbers and the hierarchy is stark: the galaxy's own internal acceleration is around a hundred-trillionth, the external field from the group is roughly ten to twenty times larger, and both sit far below a-nought. The group's field has already claimed the substrate's outer-scale capacity at that location. There is no slack left over to spend on the galaxy's internal motion. The boost does not appear, because the substrate's account is already overdrawn.

So DF2 is not a galaxy that is missing a substance. It is a galaxy whose capacity was spent by its environment before its own stars could draw on it. The very galaxy built to prove dark matter is a substance turns out, under this lens, to be exactly what the framework predicts: put a low-acceleration galaxy in a strong enough external field, and its boost switches off. Modified-gravity people have a cousin of this, the external field effect, and I want to be square that Event Density is not claiming to have invented the idea that environment matters. What it claims is a *reason* environment matters, a mechanism made of finite substrate capacity rather than a tuned interpolation function, and one sharp difference: because the capacity is a hard quantity that is either exceeded or not, the switch-off should be a knee, an abrupt turn, rather than the smooth roll-off that plain MOND naturally gives.

That difference is a live, killable prediction, and it comes with a control case attached. Find an ultra-diffuse galaxy just like DF2, same mass, same slow internal motion, but sitting alone in a void with no big neighbor. Event Density says it must show the full boost, because its slack is untouched. Dark matter says nothing in particular, since a halo should be there regardless of neighbors. Measure a population of these galaxies across a range of environments and watch for the knee. If the transition from boosted to unboosted is a smooth ramp with no threshold, this mechanism is wrong. Again the trade: a claim built to die if the data says so.

The tier here is honest to state. This is an account with order-of-magnitude numbers that work, plus a distinguishing prediction. The exact shape of the suppression, the substrate's own version of the interpolation curve, has not been derived from first principles yet. It is a strong reading with a named gap, not a closed theorem.

## Exhibit three: the gravity that walked away from the gas

Which brings us to the Bullet, the picture that gets called the smoking gun. Two clusters collided at a few thousand kilometers a second. The galaxies, which are tiny and far apart, sailed straight through each other. The gas, which fills the space between them, could not, so it collided, heated, and got left behind in the middle, and the gas holds most of the ordinary mass. Then you map the gravity by how it bends the light of background galaxies, and the gravity is not in the middle with the gas. It is out on the two sides, sitting with the galaxies. Mass in one place, gravity in another. The standard reading is immediate: an invisible collisionless component passed through with the galaxies and carried the gravity with it. Dark matter, caught in the act of separating from the baryons.

Event Density reads the same picture without a particle, and the reading is specific enough to make numbers. The substrate's organizational state at cluster scales is a field of orientations, a direction defined at every point, like the alignment inside a magnet. That kind of field can get *knotted*. It can wrap around a point in space in a way that cannot be smoothed out without tearing, the same topology that traps defects in the early universe when it cools too fast to settle. A collision at thousands of kilometers a second is exactly such a too-fast quench. It knocks the organizational field into a knotted configuration and freezes it there, and because the total amount of knotting is conserved and started at zero, the knots must come in canceling pairs, one and its mirror. That is why there are exactly two offset peaks, not one and not three. Two halves of a single pair, forced by a conservation law.

And the knots ride with the galaxies, not the gas, for the same reason the galaxies pass through cleanly: the organizational structure is carried by the collisionless component, while the gas is just matter that got mechanically stripped away and stranded. So the gravity, which in this framework is the shadow of that organizational structure, sits with the galaxies and is offset from the gas. The picture even explains why the sky is not littered with Bullets: the knot at the core is permanent, but the extended halo of field around it that actually does the lensing relaxes over roughly a billion years, so only recent mergers are still bright. The Bullet looks the way it does because it is young.

Now, does this prove anything? No, and here is the part I most want a skeptic to hear. The Bullet fits dark matter. It fits modified gravity with enough tuning. It fits this. Three suspects, one clue that points at all of them. A clue that fits everyone convicts no one. The honest response is not to claim the Bullet for Event Density; it is to find the measurement where the three suspects say *different* things. That measurement is the relationship between how fast clusters collide and how far their gravity ends up displaced from their gas, across the whole catalog of merging clusters, not just this one. Dark matter predicts no clean relationship at all, just scatter set by the messy details of each collision. Modified gravity predicts a smooth, gradual rise. Event Density predicts a specific shape with a sharp knee: nothing below a threshold speed of around a hundred and fifty kilometers a second, then linear growth, then a ceiling near the freeze-out scale, which the numbers put close to the Bullet's observed peak separation. Three different shapes. One survey of merging clusters could tell them apart.

The tier: this is a synthesis with real numerical closure, the threshold and the freeze-out scale both computed from substrate scales and both landing near the Bullet's actual numbers, but resting on a couple of substrate-physics ingredients that are still estimates rather than derivations. The offset-velocity law is the falsifiable heart of it. No knee, no ceiling, and the mechanism is wrong.

## What ties the three together

Step back and the three exhibits are one story told three ways. In each, the thing people call dark matter is the substrate's own organizational structure, and gravity is that structure's shadow. In the Bullet, the structure is *displaced* from the matter, frozen into a knot that rode off with the galaxies. In DF2, the structure's boost is *suppressed*, its capacity already spent by the group. In the rotation curves, the structure carries a *cosmic scale*, the acceleration tuned to the size of the universe. Displaced, suppressed, cosmic. A halo of particles can be displaced, and it can arguably be absent, which is exactly why those two convinced an honest skeptic. But a halo of particles has no natural way to be cosmic, to carry the Hubble constant in its switch-over acceleration. The one exhibit the particle cannot fake is the one that has been sitting quietly in the rotation curves the whole time.

So the separability that moved Don is real. The gravity genuinely does come apart from the ordinary matter, in the Bullet by displacement and in DF2 by absence. Event Density agrees completely that it comes apart. What it contests is the very next step, the inference from "separable from the gas" to "therefore a particle." In this framework the thing that separates from the matter is the substrate's structure, not a new species of matter, and that same structure is what stamps the cosmic acceleration onto every galaxy. One account, three exhibits, and it reaches for the fingerprint the particle account has to call a coincidence.

## What this is, and what it is not

Let me be as square as the essay demands. This does not refute dark matter. You cannot refute a particle with an essay, and I am not trying to. There may yet be a real particle; the search is worth running. What Event Density offers is a coherent third reading that survives the two observations built to kill modified gravity, and that pays for its survival with predictions it can be killed by. It does not derive cosmology, and it does not derive the Hubble constant; a-nought is form-forced and value-inherited, the shape fixed and the number borrowed. The DF2 mechanism is an account with a gap where its suppression curve should be. The Bullet synthesis has open ingredients under its numbers. I would rather hand you the map with the unpaved roads drawn in than sell you a finished country.

But every piece of it can be wrong, and cleanly. If a tighter Hubble constant pushes the predicted a-nought off the measured value, exhibit one dies. If isolated void galaxies do not show the full boost, or if the environmental switch-off is a smooth ramp rather than a knee, exhibit two dies. If the catalog of merging clusters shows no threshold and no ceiling in its offset-velocity relation, exhibit three dies. Set that against the particle account's genuine discomfort, which is not any single failure but a habit: dark matter is inferred wherever gravity misbehaves, its distribution fit to whatever each system needs, and the one clean number it might have owned, the cosmic acceleration scale, it can only call a coincidence.

So here is where I would leave the situational Don. You updated on DF2 and the Bullet, and you were right to. The gravity does separate from the matter; those observations are real and they do rule things out. But they rule out the *old* modified gravity, the unconditional law, and they were never a test of this. The reading on the table now is one where the extra gravity is the substrate's structure, which can be displaced and suppressed just as you saw, and which also carries the one fingerprint a particle cannot: an acceleration that knows the size of the universe. It is not asking to be believed. It is asking to be tested, and it has told you exactly which measurements would end it.
