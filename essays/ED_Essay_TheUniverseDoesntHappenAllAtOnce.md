# The Universe Doesn't Happen All at Once

*An essay on a small idea with large consequences: that becoming is rare, and that this single fact may be why there is quantum mechanics, why clocks bend, and why a theory built on the arrow of time survives its hardest test.*

**Allen Proxmire**

*Companion paper: `Paper_GR-IV_ArrowsAlibi`.*

---

Start with something you already believe but have probably never said out loud: the universe does not happen all at once.

Things become. A photon that was, a moment ago, smeared across two slits as a wave of possibility *becomes* a single dot on a screen. An undecided quantum coin *becomes* heads. These are not gentle transitions. They are commitments, sharp, irreversible, never-to-be-undone acts by which what was indeterminate becomes a fact. And the striking thing about them, the thing we rarely stop to notice, is how *rare* they are. Most of the time, most of the universe is not committing to anything. It is holding its possibilities open, coherent, superposed, undecided, and only occasionally, here and there, does a fact crystallize out.

I want to convince you that this rarity is not an incidental feature of the world. It is closer to the *condition* for there being a world at all. And I want to show you that the same rarity, taken seriously, quietly resolves a problem in gravity that should have killed a theory I've been building. The two things, the strangeness of quantum mechanics and the safety of relativity, turn out to be one thing seen twice.

## A theory with an arrow in it

Here is the setup, as briefly as I can make it honest.

Almost all of fundamental physics is written in laws that do not care which way time points. Run the equations of Newton, Maxwell, or Einstein backward, and they describe a perfectly legal world. The arrow of time, the fact that eggs break but do not unbreak, that you remember the past and not the future, is, on the standard view, *not in the laws*. It is in the boundary conditions: the universe started in a very special, low-entropy state, and everything asymmetric flows from that. The laws are timeless; the arrow is an accident of how things began.

I've spent a long time exploring the opposite choice. Suppose the most fundamental thing about the world is not a symmetric equation but an *act*, the irreversible commitment by which possibility becomes fact. Suppose the arrow is in the *law*, and the smooth, time-symmetric physics we know is an approximation several floors up, the way the smooth flow of water is an approximation to a chaos of molecules. I call the framework Event Density, because what is fundamental is not space, not particles, not fields, but the density of committed events, of *becomings*. Gravity, in this picture, is literally how thick the becoming is in a region. Where reality is committing hard, spacetime curves.

This is an unusual place to start, and I won't pretend it's confirmed. But it does something I find hard to look away from: in the regimes worked out so far, it reproduces the results of real gravity. The bending of starlight by exactly Einstein's factor of two. The slowing of clocks in a gravitational field. The strange flat rotation of galaxies that we usually blame on invisible dark matter. All of it falls out of one substrate whose only special structure is that becoming runs one way.

And it comes with a danger. A theory that singles out "becoming" singles out a *preferred frame*, a cosmic sense of rest, the frame in which the universe's becoming is, on average, even. Theories with a preferred frame are notoriously easy to kill. They predict that a planet's gravity should depend slightly on how fast the planet is moving through that cosmic frame, an effect physicists measure with exquisite precision and have never seen. The generic prediction for my kind of theory is an effect of *order one*, and the experiments say it must be smaller than a part in a hundred thousand. By the usual reckoning, my theory should have been dead on arrival.

## Why dense becoming would freeze the world

The rescue comes from the rarity of becoming, and to see it you have to ask a question that sounds like philosophy but turns out to be physics: *how often does the universe commit?*

There are two possibilities. Either commitment happens constantly, at every fundamental tick of time, everywhere, or it happens *sparsely*, only where and when some possibility actually gets resolved.

Suppose it were constant. Suppose every tick, every patch of the universe, were forced to decide. Then there could be no superposition: every quantum possibility would be collapsed to a definite answer the instant it formed. There could be no interference, no coherence, none of the wave-like undecidedness that *is* quantum mechanics. Worse: a system that is measured continuously cannot change at all. This is a real and proven effect, the quantum Zeno effect. Watch a quantum system closely enough and you freeze it in place, like a pot that won't boil because you never stop staring at it. A universe that commits at every tick is a universe under continuous measurement. It would be frozen. Nothing could evolve. There would be no dynamics, no time worth the name, and certainly no quantum mechanics.

So if the universe has quantum mechanics, and it does, then becoming *cannot* be dense. It must be sparse. The substrate must hold its possibilities open, coherent and uncommitted, across many ticks, committing only now and then, here and there. **Quantum mechanics is the universe refusing to decide everything at once.** Superposition just *is* persistent indeterminacy, a question the world has not yet answered. Interference is what that unanswered question does. Measurement, decoherence, the famous "collapse": that is commitment, the rare event when an answer finally crystallizes.

This is not a hypothesis I added to save the theory. It is forced. A theory of becoming that wants to have quantum mechanics has no choice but to make becoming rare.

## The same rarity bends clocks

Now watch the same fact do a second job.

Gravity, in Einstein's hands, is the bending of spacetime, and the most human face of it is time dilation: a clock deep in a gravitational well ticks slower than a clock far away. This is real, your phone's GPS corrects for it every second. But ask what it *means* for a clock to run slow. It means the rate of *becoming* is not the same everywhere. Time passes more slowly down there because reality is, in a precise sense, committing more slowly down there.

And here is the small, stubborn observation that started me down this whole road: **you cannot have time dilation if everything ticks the same.** A clock can only run slow relative to another clock if the rate of becoming varies from place to place. Uniform becoming, every patch committing at the identical rate, gives you a rigid, universal clock and no gravity at all. A *bending* tick is what we call gravity. So the non-uniformity of becoming, the very thing that makes commitment sparse and uneven, is also the thing that makes clocks bend. Gravitational time dilation and quantum coherence are not two phenomena. They are two readings of one fact: the universe becomes unevenly, and rarely.

## And the same rarity is the alibi

Which brings us back to the danger, the preferred-frame effect that should have killed the theory.

That effect, it turns out, is set by how *stiff* the cosmic becoming is locally, how strongly the substrate's sense of "now" resists being deformed. And that stiffness is just the local density of committed events measured against the densest it could possibly be (the Planck scale, the finest grain of reality). In empty space, where the universe is mostly coherent and rarely commits, that density is *fantastically* small, not a little small, but small by seventy, ninety, a hundred and twenty orders of magnitude. So the preferred-frame effect, which would have been of order one if becoming were dense, is instead suppressed into oblivion. The arrow is real, woven into the law, but in the data it leaves no shadow. It has an alibi.

And the alibi is coherence itself. The same sparseness of becoming that lets the universe stay quantum is exactly what makes its preferred frame too faint to detect. You cannot have one without the other. A universe that committed densely enough to show a preferred-frame effect would, by that very density, have no quantum mechanics. It would be the frozen, Zeno-locked world that cannot evolve. The fact that we *have* quantum mechanics is, in this picture, the same fact as the theory passing its hardest gravitational test.

One structure, three faces: coherence, dilation, and silence. The universe does not collapse every tick, and that single, almost trivial-sounding refusal is why it can interfere, why its clocks can bend, and why a theory built on the arrow of time survives.

## What I am, and am not, claiming

Let me be careful, because this is the part where it would be easy to oversell, and overselling is how good ideas get dismissed.

I am not claiming this is confirmed. The framework rests on postulates I have not derived from anything deeper. It inherits some numbers, Newton's constant and the cosmic expansion rate, from measurement rather than first principles. There are open computations it has not yet done and could still fail. And the central move of this essay, that becoming is sparse, I argue is *forced* by the existence of quantum mechanics, but "forced by an argument" is not the same as "proven in a lab."

It can also be killed cleanly, and I would rather hand you the knife than hide it. The framework predicts that one particular preferred-frame parameter, the one physicists call alpha-two, is *exactly* zero, not merely small. It is a forced consequence of the structure, not a dial I tuned. Any firm detection of that parameter, at any level, would end the theory outright. That is the sharpest single way it could fail, and it is already being measured.

What I am claiming is narrower and, I think, more interesting than a confirmation. I am claiming that a single idea, that the universe does not happen all at once, does an improbable amount of work. It is the condition for quantum mechanics. It is the source of time dilation. And it is the reason a preferred-frame theory, which should be dead, is instead safe by a margin so vast that no experiment could see the difference from Einstein's gravity, except possibly at the very edge of the universe, at the densities of black-hole cores and the first instant of the Big Bang, which is exactly where everyone already expects ordinary physics to break.

When one idea pays for that many things at once, I have learned to take it seriously. Not because it must be right, it may not be, but because that kind of structural economy is what *correct* ideas tend to look like. A wrong idea that happened to fix the preferred-frame problem would not also have explained why there is quantum mechanics and why clocks bend. It would have fixed the one thing and left the rest as coincidence. This fixes all three with the same stroke, and asks for nothing extra.

So here is the whole of it, small enough to carry: the universe does not become all at once. It commits rarely, and unevenly. That rarity is why anything can stay in superposition, why time can run at different speeds in different places, and why the arrow of time, which is unmistakably present in the law, leaves no fingerprint on the data. The arrow is loud where it should be, and silent where it must be. And the silence, it turns out, is the same thing as the coherence. The same mechanism that keeps the universe quantum keeps it relativistic.

That is a strange and beautiful thing to find at the bottom of a theory, and I did not put it there on purpose. I went looking for a way the theory could die, and found instead that it lives for the same reason the world does.
