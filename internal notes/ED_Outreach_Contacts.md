## §4 What an arXiv endorsement request looks like

arXiv requires endorsement for first-time submitters in most subfields. The endorser must be an active researcher who has posted to that subfield within the last 5 years. The request is *not* a peer review — the endorser is vouching that you're a real researcher producing real (not nonsense) work, not that the work is correct.

### Template (adapt freely)

**Subject:** arXiv endorsement request — substrate-graph derivation of BTFR slope-4 (gr-qc / astro-ph.CO)

> Dear Prof. [Name],
>
> I am an independent researcher writing to ask if you would be willing to consider an arXiv endorsement for me in [gr-qc / astro-ph.CO]. I have been working over the past [N] years on a substrate-ontology research program ("Event Density") and have posted [N] papers as Zenodo preprints (DOIs available); I would like to make the BTFR-slope-4 derivation paper available on arXiv to reach the modified-gravity research community where this prediction is most relevant.
>
> The paper in question (attached) derives the Baryonic Tully-Fisher Relation with slope exactly 4 and zero intrinsic scatter in the deep-MOND asymptotic regime from a substrate-level identification of the MOND transition acceleration as $a_0 = cH_0/(2\pi)$. The derivation is form-FORCED from substrate primitives; the prediction is testable against SPARC catalog data at current precision.
>
> I am attaching the paper itself (~10 pages) and a 2-page executive summary. The Zenodo DOIs for the broader corpus are at [link]. Information about the research program is at [GitHub link].
>
> I understand endorsement is not a peer review and is a vouching that the submitter is a real researcher producing serious work, not that the work is correct. If you would prefer not to endorse, that is also a fully appropriate response, and I appreciate your time either way.
>
> Best regards,
> Allen Proxmire

### Key elements

- **Subject line names the topic + subfield.** Not "please endorse my theory of everything."
- **One paragraph identifies you + work + ask.** Not a corpus dump.
- **Specific paper + executive summary attached.** Endorser shouldn't have to dig.
- **Zenodo DOI list shows you are a real producer**, not a one-off.
- **Explicit "endorsement is not peer review" disclaimer.** Shows you understand the social contract.
- **Explicit "no is also a fine answer" closer.** Reduces social friction; the endorser can decline without guilt, which paradoxically makes "yes" more likely.

### What to do with a successful endorsement

- Post the BTFR paper to arXiv immediately
- Cross-reference all Zenodo DOIs in the arXiv paper (lets readers find the broader corpus)
- Email the endorser back with the arXiv link as a thank-you (preserves the relationship for future)
- *Then* consider submitting to Foundations of Physics (with arXiv link as evidence of community engagement)

### What to do with a declined endorsement (the common case)

- Thank them
- Move to next person on list
- Don't take it personally — endorsements are declined for many reasons, most of which are not about your work

## §5 Outreach principles (for cold emails generally)

| Do | Don't |
|---|---|
| One specific paper attached | The whole 100+ paper corpus |
| 2-3 paragraph email | 2-3 page email |
| Specific ask ("would you read this?") | Vague ask ("would love your thoughts") |
| Acknowledge their time + grant easy "no" | Imply you deserve their attention |
| Reference their own work specifically | Generic "your work is important" platitudes |
| Send once, wait 4 weeks, then move on | Follow up multiple times |
| Use technical language they use | Generic "physics" language |
| Identify as independent researcher | Pretend to affiliation you don't have |
| Lead with the prediction, not the ontology | Lead with the 13 primitives |

**Counter-intuitive observation:** the BTFR-slope-4 prediction is the wedge. Don't lead with the substrate ontology. Lead with: "I have a derivation that forces BTFR slope-4 with zero intrinsic scatter; here's the substrate-level mechanism; here's the SPARC-data test." The ontology comes *after* the prediction in the reading order. MOND researchers care about predictions; the ontology behind them is interesting-to-them only after the prediction has been shown to work.

## §6 Realistic timeline expectations

- **Email sent** → typical response time: 1-4 weeks for "yes" or "no"; longer = de facto "no"
- **arXiv endorsement obtained** → can post within 24 hours
- **arXiv paper posted** → traction visible in citations / downloads at 3-6 month timescale
- **Foundations of Physics submission** → 4-8 month referee process; success rate for outsiders with arXiv presence ~20-40%
- **Substantive citation by physicist of stature** → realistic timescale 2-5 years from initial arXiv posting

Independent-researcher work in physics moves on years-scale, not months-scale. The Zenodo DOIs you already have are doing slow-compound work via search indexing + AI training corpus inclusion + occasional graduate-student discovery; the outreach amplifies that timescale but does not bypass it.
