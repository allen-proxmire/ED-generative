## 1. Framing

### 1.1 What standard quantum mechanics postulates about dynamics

Every introductory course on quantum mechanics presents the Schrödinger equation as a postulate. The state vector $\psi(t) \in \mathcal{H}$ evolves in time according to
$$
i\hbar\,\frac{d}{dt} |\psi(t)\rangle = \hat{H} |\psi(t)\rangle,
$$
where $\hat{H}$ is the Hamiltonian operator. Four structural features are bundled into this single line:

1. **Linearity.** The evolution operator acts linearly on $|\psi\rangle$. Superpositions are preserved: $|\psi(0)\rangle = \alpha|\phi_1\rangle + \beta|\phi_2\rangle$ implies $|\psi(t)\rangle = \alpha|\phi_1(t)\rangle + \beta|\phi_2(t)\rangle$.
2. **First-order in time.** The evolution involves $d/dt$, not $d^2/dt^2$ or higher.
3. **Unitarity.** The norm $\langle \psi | \psi \rangle$ is preserved under evolution. This is required for the Born rule to remain a probability assignment.
4. **Hermiticity of the generator.** The operator $\hat{H}$ is self-adjoint, with real eigenvalues identified as energies.

These four features are not independent commitments. (3) and (4) are tightly coupled (Stone's theorem connects strongly continuous one-parameter unitary groups to self-adjoint generators). (1) and (2) are also coupled (the Stone-theorem operator-exponential form $U_t = e^{-i\hat{H}t/\hbar}$ is automatically linear and first-order). But the *fact* that nature obeys a law with exactly this combination of features — rather than, say, a non-linear evolution, or a second-order-in-time wave equation, or a non-unitary collapse process — is a substantive structural commitment of quantum mechanics.

The standard presentation states all four as a single postulate ("time evolution is generated by a self-adjoint Hamiltonian via the Schrödinger equation"). Where each feature comes from, why nature insists on this specific dynamical form rather than alternatives that have been studied (Weinberg's nonlinear Schrödinger, GRW/CSL non-unitary collapse, Bohmian guidance equations), and why the constant is precisely $i\hbar$ — these are not derived in standard quantum mechanics. They are part of the package.

### 1.2 The puzzle

Several programs have tried to motivate the Schrödinger equation from deeper principles:

- **Wigner's analysis of symmetry**: time-translation symmetry on a Hilbert space, by Stone's theorem, produces a unique self-adjoint generator. This shows that *if* the dynamics is unitary on a Hilbert space, *then* it has the Schrödinger form. The Hilbert space and the unitary-evolution postulate are inputs.
- **Path-integral reconstruction** (Feynman): the Schrödinger equation emerges as the infinitesimal form of the path-integral propagator. The path-integral measure and the action principle are inputs.
- **Classical-mechanics correspondence**: the Schrödinger equation is motivated by analogy with the Hamilton-Jacobi equation in the classical limit. The Hamiltonian itself is the classical kinetic-plus-potential expression promoted to an operator.

These programs are useful, but each takes the Hilbert space, the unitary evolution, or the classical Hamiltonian as input. None derives the dynamical law from a substrate of pre-quantum structure. The deeper question — why does the world have dynamics that look like a Stone-theorem-generated unitary group, on a Hilbert space, with a Hermitian generator? — remains.

Alternatives have been proposed:

- **Weinberg's nonlinear Schrödinger** (1989): a small nonlinear term added to the standard equation. Experimentally constrained to be small but not derived to be zero.
- **GRW spontaneous-localization** (Ghirardi-Rimini-Weber 1986) and **CSL** (Continuous Spontaneous Localization, Pearle 1989): collapse-based modifications producing effectively non-unitary evolution at the level of the system state.
- **Bohmian mechanics**: a guidance equation $dq/dt = \nabla S/m$ added alongside the standard Schrödinger equation, supplying particle trajectories.

Each of these is consistent with current experimental data at appropriate parameter regimes; each modifies the Schrödinger equation in a different direction. The standard quantum-mechanical formalism remains the default because it matches experiment to extraordinary precision, but no first-principles argument forbids the alternatives.

A program that wants to settle the question structurally needs:

1. A pre-quantum substrate from which the participation manifold and inner product are derived.
2. An argument that time-translation symmetry on that substrate, plus inner-product preservation, forces the unitary one-parameter group structure.
3. An explicit exclusion of alternatives — nonlinear, higher-order, non-unitary, non-Hermitian, real-vector-space, quaternionic — by substrate-condition violations.

## 6. Alternative Encodings

### 6.1 Structural alternatives

**A1. Nonlinear evolution equations.** Evolution laws in which the time-derivative depends on $P$ in a nonlinear way: $\partial_t P = \mathcal{F}[P]$ where $\mathcal{F}$ is a nonlinear functional. Examples include $\partial_t P = \hat{H}(P)\, P$ with $\hat{H}$ depending on the state, or terms like $|P|^2 P$ that appear in classical nonlinear wave equations.

**A2. Higher-order time derivatives.** Evolution laws involving $\partial_t^2$, $\partial_t^3$, or higher derivatives. Examples: a Klein-Gordon-like wave equation $\partial_t^2 P + \hat{H}^2 P = 0$, or a Friedrichs-class third-order regularization $\partial_t^3 P = \mathcal{L}[P]$.

**A3. Non-unitary evolution.** Evolution operators $U_t$ for which the norm $\langle U_t P \mid U_t P \rangle \neq \langle P \mid P \rangle$ for some $P$. Examples: dissipative semigroups in open-system Lindblad form, exponential decay generators, classical-stochastic-process generators acting on the participation manifold.

**A4. Anti-Hermitian or non-Hermitian generators.** Generators $\hat{H}$ with $\hat{H}^\dagger = -\hat{H}$ (anti-Hermitian) or $\hat{H}^\dagger \neq \pm \hat{H}$ (non-Hermitian). The exponential of an anti-Hermitian operator gives a unitary one-parameter group with imaginary spectrum; non-Hermitian generators produce non-unitary evolution.

**A5. Discontinuous or non-differentiable evolution.** Evolution laws in which $t \mapsto P(t)$ is not strongly continuous, or in which the operator-domain orbit $t \mapsto U_t P$ is not differentiable for $P$ in the domain of $\hat{H}$. Examples: piecewise-constant evolutions, jump processes, evolutions defined only on a dense subset of times.

**A6. Real-valued or quaternionic evolution laws.** Evolution equations formulated on a real-valued or quaternionic carrier rather than the complex participation manifold. Examples: real-vector-space evolution with auxiliary $J$-operator; quaternionic time-evolution semigroups.

**A7. Evolution laws violating $U(1)$ structure.** Evolution laws in which the global $U(1)$ gauge $P \to e^{i\alpha} P$ is not preserved — that is, $U_t$ does not commute with the global phase rotation.

### 6.2 Mainstream alternatives

**B1. GRW / CSL stochastic dynamics.** Spontaneous-localization models (Ghirardi-Rimini-Weber 1986; Pearle 1989) introduce a stochastic non-unitary collapse term producing effectively non-linear, non-unitary evolution at the level of single-system trajectories. Standard Schrödinger evolution is recovered in the average; departures appear at the characteristic GRW/CSL rates.

**B2. Bohmian guidance equation.** Bohmian mechanics retains the standard Schrödinger equation for $\psi$ but adds a guidance equation $dq/dt = \text{Im}(\nabla\psi/\psi)\hbar/m$ for hidden particle trajectories. The Schrödinger equation itself is unmodified; the alternative is at the level of supplementary dynamical structure, not the wavefunction's evolution.

**B3. Weinberg nonlinear Schrödinger.** Weinberg (1989) proposed a small nonlinear modification of the standard Schrödinger equation, parametrized by coupling constants experimentally constrained but not derived to be zero.

**B4. Collapse models with non-unitary generators.** Variants of GRW/CSL using explicit non-unitary generators (e.g., $\hat{H} = \hat{H}_0 - i\hat{\Gamma}$ with $\hat{\Gamma}$ a positive damping operator). Used in some open-system descriptions; not consistent with closed-system unitarity.

**B5. Real-vector-space QM dynamics (Stueckelberg-class).** Real-vector-space variants of quantum mechanics use a two-component real state $\Psi \in \mathbb{R}^{2}$ with auxiliary $J$-operator. The dynamical equation is the standard Schrödinger equation written in real-vector notation; under $J = i$ identification, it reduces to the complex form.

**B6. Quaternionic QM dynamics (Adler-class).** Quaternionic quantum mechanics uses a quaternion-valued state with quaternionic time-evolution semigroup. Internally consistent for single-particle non-relativistic systems but produces composition pathologies in multi-particle settings.

### 7.1 Existence of a strongly continuous one-parameter unitary group

**Intuition.** Time homogeneity (C5) is the substrate-level statement that no instant is privileged. This translates directly into a one-parameter family of operators on the participation manifold — the time-translation operators — that shift states forward or backward in time by a parameter $t$. Inner-product preservation (from Paper #3 and the bandwidth-conservation feature of C2) makes these operators unitary.

**Definition.** Time homogeneity (C5) provides a one-parameter $\mathbb{R}$-action on the substrate's structural content: for each $t \in \mathbb{R}$, the substrate admits a structural automorphism $\phi_t$ that shifts the participation graph by $t$ along the continuous time axis. This automorphism lifts to a linear operator $U_t : \mathcal{H} \to \mathcal{H}$ on the participation manifold, with $U_t P$ defined as the participation-measure state corresponding to $\phi_t$-shifted substrate content. The definition is representation-independent: $U_t$ is the structural action induced by time homogeneity on the participation manifold, not a coordinate transformation of any explicit time variable inside $P_K(u)$.

**Group property.** From the additive structure of $\mathbb{R}$, $\phi_t \circ \phi_s = \phi_{t+s}$: therefore $U_t \circ U_s = U_{t+s}$, $U_0 = \mathbb{1}$, and $U_t^{-1} = U_{-t}$. The family $\{U_t\}_{t \in \mathbb{R}}$ forms an abelian one-parameter group.

**Unitarity.** Time homogeneity (C5) leaves the substrate's structural facts invariant: bandwidth $b_K(u)$ at each locus, the inner-product structure of the participation manifold, and the participation-measure carrier are all preserved under $\phi_t$. Therefore
$$
\langle U_t P \mid U_t Q \rangle = \langle P \mid Q \rangle

for all $P, Q \in \mathcal{H}$. Each $U_t$ is unitary: $U_t^\dagger U_t = \mathbb{1}$.

**Equivalent route — unitarity from bandwidth conservation.** The same conclusion follows directly from C2: bandwidth conservation along an isolated chain's persistence regime gives $\sum_K \sum_u b_K(u)$ constant under time evolution. Combined with the inner-product identity $\langle P \mid P \rangle = \sum_K \sum_u b_K(u)$ from Paper #3, this is precisely norm preservation $\langle U_t P \mid U_t P \rangle = \langle P \mid P \rangle$, which by polarization extends to all $P, Q$ and is exactly unitarity of $U_t$.

**Strong continuity.** $t \mapsto U_t P$ is strongly continuous for every $P \in \mathcal{H}$. This follows from the $L^2$-continuity of translation operators on the time variable, plus the substrate-level continuous-time interpretation of Primitive 13 (C5). Explicitly, for any $P \in \mathcal{H}$ and $\epsilon > 0$, there exists $\delta > 0$ such that $|t - t'| < \delta$ implies $\|U_t P - U_{t'} P\| < \epsilon$.

The family $\{U_t\}_{t \in \mathbb{R}}$ is therefore a strongly continuous one-parameter abelian unitary group on $\mathcal{H}$.

### 7.2 Unique self-adjoint generator (Stone's theorem)

**Intuition.** Stone's theorem is not a dynamical assumption. It is a structural fact about unitary representations of time translations on complex Hilbert spaces: once a strongly continuous one-parameter unitary group exists, a unique self-adjoint generator necessarily follows. The dynamical content has already been established by §7.1 (the existence of the unitary group from substrate primitives); Stone's theorem now converts that existence into operator form.

By Stone's theorem (1932), every strongly continuous one-parameter unitary group on a complex Hilbert space has a unique self-adjoint generator. Applied to $\{U_t\}$:

> There exists a unique self-adjoint operator $\hat{H}$ on $\mathcal{H}$ such that
> $$
> U_t = e^{-i\hat{H}t/\hbar}, \qquad t \in \mathbb{R}.
> $$
> The domain of $\hat{H}$ is dense in $\mathcal{H}$; the operator is self-adjoint on its domain: $\hat{H}^\dagger = \hat{H}$.

The factor $\hbar$ in the exponential is a unit convention — equivalent to a rescaling of $\hat{H}$ — and is inherited from the Madelung anchoring of Papers #1-#3. The structural content of Stone's theorem is the existence and uniqueness of the self-adjoint generator; $\hbar$ enters as a dimensional conversion between time and the spectrum of $\hat{H}$.

**Hermiticity is FORCED.** Stone's theorem produces a self-adjoint generator automatically. Non-Hermitian generators would correspond to non-unitary one-parameter groups, which contradict the unitarity established in §7.1 (which itself follows from inner-product preservation, which itself follows from bandwidth conservation and the substrate-level inner product of Paper #3). The chain is closed: Hermiticity is forced.

### 7.3 Linearity (automatic from operator structure)

The operator $\hat{H}$ is, by Stone's theorem, a linear operator on its domain. Therefore the evolution equation
$$
\partial_t U_t P = -\frac{i}{\hbar}\hat{H} U_t P
$$
is linear: if $P_1(t)$ and $P_2(t)$ are solutions, so is any complex linear combination $\alpha P_1(t) + \beta P_2(t)$.

**Linearity is FORCED.** It is automatic from the operator-on-Hilbert-space structure that Stone's theorem produces. Nonlinear evolution laws cannot arise from the operator-exponential of a self-adjoint generator on a Hilbert space; they would require either (i) a generator depending on the state (violating Stone's theorem's hypothesis of a fixed self-adjoint operator) or (ii) a different time-evolution structure not generated by a one-parameter unitary group (violating the unitarity established in §7.1).

### 7.4 First-order-in-time structure (automatic from operator-exponential)

Differentiating the operator-exponential identity $U_t = e^{-i\hat{H}t/\hbar}$ with respect to $t$:
$$
\frac{d}{dt} U_t = -\frac{i}{\hbar} \hat{H} \, U_t.
$$
Applied to a state $P$:
$$
\frac{d}{dt} (U_t P) = -\frac{i}{\hbar} \hat{H} (U_t P).
$$
Writing $P(t) := U_t P(0)$:
$$
i\hbar\,\partial_t P(t) = \hat{H}\,P(t).
$$
This is the Schrödinger equation in skeleton form: linear, first-order in $\partial_t$, with self-adjoint generator $\hat{H}$.

**First-order structure is FORCED.** It is the direct consequence of differentiating the operator-exponential once. Higher-order evolution laws would require either (i) a different functional form for $U_t$ (not the Stone-theorem operator-exponential), violating Stone's theorem on the time-translation group; or (ii) an additional structural commitment (an extra initial condition like $\partial_t P(0)$, alongside $P(0)$, to make a second-order Cauchy problem well-posed). Neither is supplied by the substrate.

### 7.5 Schrödinger evolution — the composite result

Combining §§7.1-7.4: the unique time-evolution law on the participation manifold is the linear unitary one-parameter group $\{U_t = e^{-i\hat{H}t/\hbar}\}_{t \in \mathbb{R}}$ whose differential form is

i\hbar\,\partial_t P(t) = \hat{H}\,P(t),

## 8. Exclusion Arguments

This section dispatches each alternative encoding listed in §6.

### 8.1 A1 — Nonlinear evolution equations

**Claim.** Nonlinear evolution laws violate the substrate-level bandwidth additivity (C2) when extended to superpositions, and violate the operator-on-Hilbert-space structure produced by Stone's theorem (§7.2).

**Argument.** Bandwidth additivity (C2) requires that for disjoint participation contributions $P = P_1 + P_2$ (orthogonal supports), the total bandwidth $\langle P \mid P \rangle = \langle P_1 \mid P_1 \rangle + \langle P_2 \mid P_2 \rangle$. Under time evolution, this additivity must be preserved: $\langle U_t P \mid U_t P \rangle = \langle P \mid P \rangle$ for each disjoint piece, and the orthogonality of supports must persist. Unitary linear evolution satisfies this automatically (orthogonal subspaces map to orthogonal subspaces under unitary transformations).

A nonlinear evolution $\partial_t P = \mathcal{F}[P]$ with $\mathcal{F}$ depending on $P$ in a nonlinear way fails the superposition test: $\mathcal{F}[\alpha P_1 + \beta P_2] \neq \alpha \mathcal{F}[P_1] + \beta \mathcal{F}[P_2]$ in general. The structural identity $U_t(P_1 + P_2) = U_t P_1 + U_t P_2$ does not hold, and bandwidth conservation across disjoint participation pieces fails as a consequence: the total bandwidth of the sum is no longer the sum of bandwidths after evolution.

Equivalently: Stone's theorem (§7.2) produces a linear self-adjoint generator on the Hilbert space. A nonlinear evolution is not generated by any such operator. C2 (bandwidth additivity under time evolution) and §7.2 (linear-operator structure) are both violated.

### 8.2 A2 — Higher-order time derivatives

**Claim.** Higher-order evolution laws require structural inputs (additional initial conditions) not supplied by the substrate, and violate the operator-exponential structure produced by Stone's theorem.

**Argument.** Stone's theorem on the time-translation group $\{U_t\}$ gives $U_t = e^{-i\hat{H}t/\hbar}$, differentiable once to produce a first-order equation $i\hbar\,\partial_t P = \hat{H} P$. The time-translation group has *one* parameter ($t$), and Stone's theorem produces *one* generator. There is no structural slot for a second initial condition.

Higher-order equations like the Klein-Gordon $\partial_t^2 P + \hat{H}^2 P = 0$ arise in relativistic settings where the time and space derivatives are constrained by Lorentz covariance to enter symmetrically. ### 8.3 A3 — Non-unitary evolution

**Claim.** Non-unitary evolution violates the Born rule consistency (C6 from Paper #2) and bandwidth conservation (C2).

**Argument.** The Born rule (Paper #2) gives the probability of a commitment-event outcome as $\text{Prob}(K \mid u) = |P_K(u)|^2 / \sum_{K'} |P_{K'}(u)|^2$. For this to remain a probability assignment over time, the denominator $\sum_{K'} |P_{K'}|^2 = \langle P \mid P \rangle$ must be preserved under evolution. Equivalently, the norm must be conserved: $\langle U_t P \mid U_t P \rangle = \langle P \mid P \rangle$.

Norm preservation is exactly unitarity: $U_t^\dagger U_t = \mathbb{1}$. Any non-unitary evolution would either decrease the norm (probability "leakage" out of the system) or increase it (spontaneous "creation" of probability), both of which violate the Born-rule structure that Paper #2 establishes.

Equivalently: bandwidth conservation (C2) requires $\sum_K \sum_u b_K(u) = \sum_K \sum_u |P_K(u)|^2 = \langle P \mid P \rangle$ to be conserved along an isolated chain's persistence regime. Non-unitary evolution violates bandwidth conservation directly.

GRW/CSL models (B1) and non-unitary collapse generators (B4) bypass this by introducing a separate collapse mechanism that resets the state in addition to evolving it; the unitary part of their dynamics still satisfies §7, while the collapse part is treated separately. This places them outside the substrate-conditions test: they are not unitary-evolution alternatives but rather supplementary mechanisms whose unitary parts are governed by the present forcing argument.

## Appendix A — Full Derivation Chain and Glossary

### A.1 Linearity from substrate-level bandwidth additivity

The participation manifold $\mathcal{P}$ is a complex vector space by the participation-measure construction of Paper #1: the carrier algebra is $\mathbb{C}$, and addition + complex-scalar multiplication of participation-measure arrays are well-defined operations producing other participation-measure arrays.

For disjoint participation contributions $P = P_1 + P_2$ (supports orthogonal under the inner product of Paper #3), bandwidth additivity gives
$$
\langle P \mid P \rangle = \sum_K \sum_u |P_K(u)|^2 = \sum_K \sum_u |P_{1,K}(u)|^2 + \sum_K \sum_u |P_{2,K}(u)|^2 = \langle P_1 \mid P_1 \rangle + \langle P_2 \mid P_2 \rangle.
$$
Under time evolution, this additivity is preserved if and only if the evolution operator is linear: $U_t(P_1 + P_2) = U_t P_1 + U_t P_2$. A nonlinear evolution would break the additivity at $t > 0$ even if it holds at $t = 0$.

Equivalently: the operator-exponential $U_t = e^{-i\hat{H}t/\hbar}$ of any self-adjoint operator $\hat{H}$ on a Hilbert space is automatically linear. Stone's theorem applied to the time-translation group produces such an operator, and the linearity of the generated evolution is automatic.

### A.2 Unitarity from inner-product preservation

The time-translation operator $U_t$ acts on the time variable: $(U_t P)_K(u, s) = P_K(u, s - t)$. The inner product is
$$
\langle U_t P \mid U_t Q \rangle = \sum_K \sum_u \sum_s P_K^*(u, s-t)\, Q_K(u, s-t).
$$
Substituting $s' = s - t$ in the time index sum (the sum is over a continuous time variable in the continuum regime, or a discrete time index in the discrete regime), the sum is unchanged:
$$
\langle U_t P \mid U_t Q \rangle = \sum_K \sum_u \sum_{s'} P_K^*(u, s')\, Q_K(u, s') = \langle P \mid Q \rangle.
$$
Time-translation invariance of the inner product is therefore equivalent to time homogeneity of the substrate (C5). Unitarity of $U_t$ — defined as $U_t^\dagger U_t = \mathbb{1}$, equivalently $\langle U_t P \mid U_t Q \rangle = \langle P \mid Q \rangle$ for all $P, Q$ — follows immediately.

Substrate-level bandwidth conservation (C2) provides an alternative route: $\langle P \mid P \rangle = \sum_K \sum_u b_K(u)$ is conserved along an isolated chain's persistence regime, which is the same statement as norm preservation.

### A.3 Hermiticity from Stone's theorem applied to time translations

Stone's theorem (Stone 1932, *Annals of Mathematics*): every strongly continuous one-parameter unitary group $\{U_t\}_{t \in \mathbb{R}}$ on a complex Hilbert space $\mathcal{H}$ has a unique self-adjoint generator $\hat{H}$ such that
$$
U_t = e^{-i\hat{H}t/\hbar} \qquad \text{for all } t \in \mathbb{R}.
$$
The generator is densely-defined and self-adjoint on its domain:
$$
\hat{H} = \lim_{t \to 0} \frac{i\hbar}{t}(U_t - \mathbb{1})
$$
on the dense subspace where this limit exists in the strong operator topology.

Applied to the time-translation group $\{U_t\}$ of §7.1: the group is strongly continuous (by C5's continuous-time interpretation) and consists of unitary operators (by §7.1's inner-product-preservation argument). The hypotheses of Stone's theorem are satisfied. The generator $\hat{H}$ exists, is self-adjoint, and is unique.

**Hermiticity is therefore FORCED** — it is the structural property of the generator produced by Stone's theorem applied to the substrate-derived unitary group.

### A.4 The Schrödinger equation as differential form

Differentiating the operator-exponential identity:
$$
\frac{d}{dt} U_t = -\frac{i}{\hbar}\hat{H}\,U_t.
$$
Acting on a state $P(0) \in \text{dom}(\hat{H})$ (the domain of $\hat{H}$, a dense subspace of $\mathcal{H}$), and writing $P(t) := U_t P(0)$:
$$
\frac{d P(t)}{dt} = -\frac{i}{\hbar}\hat{H}\,P(t).
$$
Multiplying both sides by $i\hbar$:
$$
i\hbar\,\frac{d P(t)}{dt} = \hat{H}\,P(t).
$$
This is the Schrödinger equation in its standard form. Linear (since $\hat{H}$ is a linear operator), first-order in $\partial_t$ (the differentiation is applied once), unitary (since $U_t$ is unitary by Stone's theorem), with self-adjoint generator (Hermiticity of $\hat{H}$).
