## 1. Framing

### 1.1 What standard physics assumes about black holes

Standard general relativity describes a black hole as a region of spacetime bounded by an **event horizon** — a one-way causal surface from beyond which no signal can escape. Inside lies a **singularity** where curvature diverges and the manifold ends. Outside, distant observers see a **thermal flux of Hawking radiation** at temperature $T_H = \hbar c^3/(8\pi G M k_B)$ — derived by Hawking (1974, 1975) from quantum field theory on the Schwarzschild background.

- **The information paradox**: Hawking's calculation predicts thermal emission, which carries no information about the matter that formed the hole; this conflicts with quantum-mechanical unitarity.
- **The Page curve**: information should reappear in the radiation when the black hole has emitted half its entropy (Page 1993); the standard semiclassical calculation does not produce this.
- **The trans-Planckian problem**: Hawking's derivation requires extrapolation of matter-field modes to arbitrarily high frequencies, where the semiclassical approximation must fail.
