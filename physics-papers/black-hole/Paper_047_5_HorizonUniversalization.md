## §3 Derivation

### 3.1 The four standardly-distinguished horizon types

**Black-hole event horizon.** For a Schwarzschild black hole of mass $M$, the event horizon is the null hypersurface at $r = 2GM/c^2$. Observers crossing it cannot communicate back to spatial infinity. Surface gravity $\kappa = c^4/(4GM)$.

**Rindler horizon.** For a uniformly-accelerated observer in Minkowski spacetime, the Rindler horizon is the null hypersurface at coordinate distance $\xi_R = c^2/\alpha$ behind the observer. Causally inaccessible region for the accelerated observer. Surface gravity $\kappa_R = \alpha$.

**Cosmological de-Sitter horizon.** In de Sitter spacetime, the cosmological horizon is at $R_H = c/H_0$ for the comoving observer. Spatial regions beyond $R_H$ recede causally; communication ceases. Surface gravity $\kappa_{dS} = c H_0$.

**Acoustic/dumb horizon.** In a fluid where flow velocity reaches the local sound speed $c_s$, the surface where $v = c_s$ acts as an acoustic horizon: sound waves cannot propagate upstream past this surface. Surface gravity $\kappa_{\mathrm{ac}} = (1/2)|dv^2/dx|$ at the horizon.
