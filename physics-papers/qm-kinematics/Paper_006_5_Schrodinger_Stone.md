## §3 Derivation

### 3.1 Substrate time-translation as one-parameter unitary group

By P13 (time homogeneity), the substrate is time-translation-invariant: substrate dynamics at time $t + s$ are related to substrate dynamics at time $t$ by a translation. By P11 (commitment-irreversibility), the translation is **forward-directional** — past states cannot be recovered from future states alone (per Paper_093 T18 kernel-level arrow of time).

For pure-state amplitude evolution, the time-translation acts as a **unitary** operator (preserves the inner-product norm; Paper_U2_Continuum):
$$\Psi(t + s) = U(s) \Psi(t) , \qquad U(s) : \mathcal{H} \to \mathcal{H} \text{ unitary} .$$

The set $\{U(s) : s \in \mathbb{R}_+\}$ (forward-time) forms a one-parameter semigroup. For the substrate's full structure (assuming reversibility on closed systems), this extends to a one-parameter unitary **group** $\{U(s) : s \in \mathbb{R}\}$.

### 3.2 Stone's theorem application

Standard **Stone's theorem** (I-Stone): every strongly-continuous one-parameter unitary group $\{U(s)\}$ on a Hilbert space has the form
$$U(s) = e^{-i\hat{A}s/\hbar}$$
for some self-adjoint operator $\hat{A}$ (the infinitesimal generator). Conversely, every self-adjoint operator generates a one-parameter unitary group via this exponentiation.

Applied to the substrate time-translation group:
$$U(t) = e^{-i\hat{H}t/\hbar} ,$$
where $\hat{H}$ is the self-adjoint generator — the **Hamiltonian**. The factor of $1/\hbar$ arises from the dimensional requirement: $\hat{H}$ has dimensions of energy, $t$ has dimensions of time, so $\hat{H}t/\hbar$ is dimensionless (Paper_RQM_hbar supplies $\hbar$ as the dimensional bridge).

### 3.3 Schrödinger equation as infinitesimal form

Differentiating $\Psi(t+s) = U(s)\Psi(t)$ at $s = 0$:
$$\frac{\partial \Psi}{\partial t} = \left.\frac{d U(s)}{ds}\right|_{s=0} \Psi = \left.\frac{d}{ds}\left(e^{-i\hat{H}s/\hbar}\right)\right|_{s=0} \Psi = -\frac{i\hat{H}}{\hbar} \Psi .$$

Rearranging:
$$i\hbar \frac{\partial \Psi}{\partial t} = \hat{H} \Psi .$$

This is the **Schrödinger equation**. The substrate-level derivation rests on Stone's theorem applied to the substrate time-translation group, with $\hbar$ supplied by Paper_RQM_hbar.

### 3.4 Why $\hat{H}$ is the Hamiltonian (energy operator)

By P13 (time homogeneity) + Noether's theorem (standard math): the conserved quantity associated with time-translation symmetry is energy. The generator of time-translation is therefore the energy operator — the Hamiltonian.

The substrate-level statement: $\hat{H}$ in $U(t) = e^{-i\hat{H}t/\hbar}$ is the substrate-level energy operator. Its eigenvalues $E_n$ are the substrate-level energy spectrum.

### 3.5 Self-adjointness of $\hat{H}$

By Stone's theorem, $\hat{H}$ is **self-adjoint** (not merely Hermitian — domain conditions matter). Self-adjointness ensures:
- Real eigenvalues (energy is real).
- Spectral resolution $\hat{H} = \int E \, dP_E$ via spectral theorem.
- Unitary exponentiation $e^{-i\hat{H}t/\hbar}$ is well-defined.

The substrate origin of self-adjointness: P11 (commitment-irreversibility) supplies forward-time evolution that is **norm-preserving** (no probability gain or loss); norm-preservation forces unitarity of $U(t)$; unitarity of the one-parameter group forces self-adjointness of the generator (Stone's theorem).

### 3.6 Connection to canonical slot 6 (V1 kernel propagation)

Paper_006 (canonical slot 6) derives unitary evolution from V1 kernel propagation. This paper supplies the **Schrödinger-equation form** of that unitary evolution.

The two are consistent: V1 kernel propagation (Paper_089) coarse-grains under DCGT to a unitary time-evolution group whose generator is $\hat{H}$. Different physical systems have different Hamiltonians (free particle: $\hat{H} = \hat{p}^2/2m$; harmonic oscillator: $\hat{H} = \hat{p}^2/2m + \tfrac{1}{2}m\omega^2 \hat{x}^2$; etc.), but the Schrödinger-equation form is universal.
