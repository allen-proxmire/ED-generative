## §1 — The shared conviction

Standard quantum mechanics leaves the collapse of the wavefunction outside its own equations. Unitary evolution is smooth and deterministic; the definite outcome of a measurement is not; and the theory bridges the two only by invoking an "observer" or a "measurement," which is a placeholder, not a mechanism. A minority of physicists have refused that placeholder and asked what actually makes an outcome definite. A striking fraction of them arrive at the same suspect: **gravity.**

The reasoning is not arbitrary. Gravity is the one interaction that couples to *everything* and grows with mass, so it is the natural candidate for a process that leaves microscopic superpositions alone (we see interference for electrons) but destroys macroscopic ones (we never see a cat in two places). And gravity is where quantum mechanics and general relativity are known to be incompatible, so it is where a *new* effect, beyond textbook QM, is most expected.

Diósi, Penrose, Hossenfelder, and Event Density all share two convictions that set them apart from the mainstream:

- **Geometry is not an independent field waiting to be quantized.** This is the quiet radical move. Mainstream quantum gravity quantizes the metric as one more field. Diósi refuses — he treats the Newtonian potential as a classical field with a fundamental indeterminacy. Penrose keeps the geometry semiclassical, so that *the mass distribution is the spacetime*. Hossenfelder goes furthest, making geometry carry no degrees of freedom of its own: matter and geometry are the *same* quantum state. Event Density is on exactly this side of the line, and arguably furthest of all: geometry in ED is **emergent**, `g ~ 1/b`, the coarse-grained shadow of the substrate's participation density, never a primitive field.
- **Collapse is physical, and it is gravity's doing.** Not an update of an observer's knowledge; a real event in the world, rooted in gravity or geometry, that would happen in an empty universe with no observers in it.

Those two commitments are the common ground. The rest of this note is where ED, having entered the room agreeing, then says something the others do not.

**Shared:** the refusal to quantize gravity, and a universal, mass-scaled, physical decoherence. **Divergence:** Diósi's collapse is a stochastic *field* injected as an addition to quantum mechanics — a noise term bolted onto the Schrödinger equation. ### 3.2 Penrose (1996): the ill-defined time, and ED's answer to it

Penrose's is the deepest version, and it is the one that touches ED's nerve directly. Take a lump of material in a quantum superposition of two locations. Each location, on its own, is a stationary state with a well-defined mass distribution and therefore a well-defined spacetime geometry. The superposition is thus a superposition of **two different spacetimes**. Now ask for the state's energy, i.e. the generator of time-translation. In general relativity, by diffeomorphism invariance, there is no coordinate-independent way to identify the points of one spacetime with the points of the other — so there is **no well-defined time-translation operator for the superposed state.** That ill-definedness is an irreducible uncertainty in the state's energy, which in the Newtonian limit equals `E_Δ`, the gravitational self-energy of the *difference* between the two mass distributions. By the energy–time relation, the superposition is not stationary after all: it decays, like an unstable particle, in a lifetime

$$T \approx \frac{\hbar}{E_\Delta}.$$

Penrose is careful that this is not a theory of collapse, only an argument for the *level* at which gravity must force deviations from unitary quantum mechanics; and he argues (against Diósi) that his version should conserve energy, protected by general covariance.

Here is the ED point, and it is the sharpest paragraph in this note. **Penrose's mechanism is the ill-definedness of a preferred time.** His superposition collapses *because* there is no fact about which time is flowing across the two superposed spacetimes. Event Density's entire foundation is that there **is** such a fact: the arrow, the substrate's direction of commitment, is a primitive preferred time — and (Report §6) it is *the same* preferred time gravity's khronon foliation picks out. So ED does not stand beside Penrose as a rival; **ED supplies exactly the object whose absence drives his argument.** Read Penrose forward: "there is no well-defined time across the superposition, therefore it must reduce." Read ED: "there is a well-defined committed time — the arrow — and the commitment in that time *is* the reduction." ED locates the collapse in the **time** itself (the committed arrow), one step *upstream* of the **energy** `E_Δ` that Penrose derives as the time-ambiguity's consequence. Penrose finds the collapse in the energy uncertainty; ED finds it in the resolution of the very time-ambiguity Penrose starts from.

This is why the resonance is not decorative. The two accounts are about the *same variable* — the preferred time of a superposed gravitational configuration — approached from opposite directions: Penrose from its absence in GR, ED from its presence in the substrate.
