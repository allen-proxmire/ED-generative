# State Reduction in ED — a plain-language summary

*A non-technical walkthrough of the ED state-reduction arc. The peer-facing synthesis is [`Paper_ED_StateReduction_vs_CollapseModels.md`](Paper_ED_StateReduction_vs_CollapseModels.md); the worked details are in the companion notes in this folder.*

## What we were doing

Quantum things can be in many places at once, but when you look, you find just one. Why the "many" becomes "one" — collapse of the wavefunction — is the oldest unsolved puzzle in quantum mechanics. A small camp of physicists thinks **gravity** is what forces the choice. We set out to place ED next to the two leading members of that camp (Diósi and Penrose) and say honestly where ED agrees, where it goes deeper, and where it still owes them something.

## ED's one big idea

For those three, gravity *causes* collapse. ED says something one step deeper: the thing that makes outcomes definite (measurement) and the thing that is gravity's flow of time are **the same single ingredient** — what ED calls the arrow, the one-way act of "committing" to a fact. So collapse and gravity's time aren't cause-and-effect; they're two faces of one thing. In particular, ED *hands Penrose the missing piece his own argument needs* (a well-defined "which clock is ticking"), which is why ED reads more as completing him than competing with him.

## The four things we worked through

1. **A collapse rate.** Picture the object's two versions running tiny internal clocks at slightly different speeds. The single arrow can't hold both, so it commits. That reproduces the same collapse-time formula the rivals use. Honest limit: it's a scaling argument that borrows the energy scale rather than computing it from scratch.

2. **The make-or-break experiment.** In 2020, an underground detector killed the simplest gravity-collapse model by looking for faint X-rays it *must* emit and finding none. Does ED emit them too? **No** — and for a clean reason. The radiating models work by constantly jiggling every particle's position; ED's collapse instead *locks* a particle into a definite state and then stops. A settled particle sits still and doesn't radiate. So ED survives exactly where the simplest rival died.

3. **A tempting new prediction that didn't survive.** ED's gravity is secretly MOND (the galaxy-rotation tweak), and tiny lab masses have such feeble gravity that they'd sit in the MOND regime — which naively would make collapse 5–50× faster than expected. Exciting. But we computed ED's own "external field effect" and it *closes the loophole*: Earth's gravity swamps the object's own feeble gravity and forces normal behavior. So in any real lab, ED matches the standard prediction. We banked that plainly as "no new weapon" instead of overselling it — that's the honest, and correct, answer.

4. **The last worry.** The "locking" argument only works if a particle settles into *one* coarse state, not a zillion microscopic ones (if it were the latter, ED would radiate and be ruled out). We showed ED's states are genuinely coarse — set by what the environment can actually tell apart — not fine-grained. So the argument holds.

## The check

An adversarial referee reviewed it twice. First pass: "major revisions" — it caught me overclaiming (I'd oversold the experiment result). We fixed every point. Second pass: "minor revisions," and it independently re-checked the key algebra and the corpus citations before signing off. Clean.

## Where it landed — honestly

ED is a **coherent, internally consistent member of the gravity-collapse family**, with a deeper reason than its rivals (one ingredient wearing two hats), and it **survives the experiment that killed the simplest competitor**. What it does *not* yet have is a brand-new testable prediction that beats the others — the one exciting candidate got closed by ED's own physics. So the result is real but modest: a strong positioning and a consistency win, not a smoking gun.

The nice part is that every place it could have failed, we found, named, and closed — so what's left is only optional polish, not doubt.
